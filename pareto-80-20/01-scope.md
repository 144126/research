# Phase 1: SCOPE — Research Framing

## Research Question
What are the most natural, logical, and causally grounded explanations for why the 80/20 (Pareto) principle appears across so many natural, social, and economic systems? What mechanisms produce this lopsided distribution, and when does it genuinely hold vs. when is it a statistical artifact or oversimplification?

## Core Components Decomposed
1. Mathematical foundations: Pareto distribution, power laws, Pareto index α = log₄5 ≈ 1.16
2. Causal mechanisms generating power-law-like distributions
3. Empirical domains where 80/20 genuinely appears
4. Statistical artifacts and misapplications
5. Criticisms, controversies, and contrarian perspectives
6. Practical guidance for when to trust vs. doubt

## Scope Boundaries
### In Scope
- Mathematical foundations: Pareto distribution, power laws, α parameter, Zipf's law
- Causal mechanisms (6+): preferential attachment, multiplicative processes, self-organized criticality, bottlenecks/diminishing returns, scale invariance/fractals, Yule-Simon process
- Natural phenomena: earthquake magnitudes, city sizes, species abundance, forest fires, word frequencies
- Social/economic: wealth, citations, web links, sales, software bugs
- Distinguishing power laws from log-normal and other skewed distributions
- Criticisms: oversimplification, arbitrary cutoffs, confirmation bias, trivial many neglected
- Historical: Pareto (1896), Juran (1941), Yule (1925), Simon (1955), Price (1976), Barabási-Albert (1999)

### Out of Scope
- Pareto efficiency (resource allocation concept)
- Detailed company case studies of 80/20 application
- Step-by-step Pareto analysis as a quality tool
- Biography of Pareto beyond his discovery

## Key Assumptions
1. Power-law distributions in natural systems share causal mechanisms with social/economic ones
2. The 80/20 ratio (α ≈ 1.16) is one point on a continuum, not a universal constant
3. Multiple causal mechanisms can produce similar-looking distributions
4. The distinction between genuine power law and log-normal is empirically difficult
