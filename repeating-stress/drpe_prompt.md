# DRPE Prompt: Why we feel stressed when we have to repeat ourselves

## Research Question
What are the psychological, neurological, and evolutionary mechanisms that cause stress, frustration, or irritation when a person has to repeat themselves — and what explains the parallel stress of being subjected to repetitive speech from others?

## Audience & Purpose
General reader seeking self-understanding. Also useful for therapists, managers, and educators. Informs communication habits and emotional regulation.

## Scope
**In:** cognitive psychology, social psychology, neuroscience of frustration, trauma responses (JADE), communication fatigue, evolutionary signaling, status/respect perception, cognitive load theory, neurodivergence (ASD, ADHD, OCD), aging and hearing loss dynamics, workplace and customer-service contexts.
**Out:** clinical treatment protocols, pharmaceutical interventions, purely linguistic analysis, non-human animal studies, theological perspectives.
**Timeframe:** classic studies (1950s–present), with emphasis on 2010s–2026 research.
**Geography:** global, primarily WEIRD populations but with attention to cultural variation.

## Dimensions (all 13 required)

### 1. Technical
- What specific neural circuits activate during unwanted repetition (anterior cingulate cortex, insula, amygdala)?
- How does cognitive load theory explain frustration when repeating?
- What is the role of the default mode network in repetitive speech?

### 2. Historical
- How did early psychologists (Freud, James, Skinner) conceptualize repetition and frustration?
- When did research on "repetition fatigue" or "repeating annoyance" first appear in literature?
- Historical evolution of the concept from "annoyance" to "cognitive overload" to "trauma response."

### 3. State-of-art
- What does current research (2020–2026) say about the stress of repeating oneself?
- Key studies on the JADE (Justify, Argue, Defend, Explain) trauma response.
- Vodafone/Jo Hemmings 2025 "repeating fatigue" research and similar industry studies.
- fMRI and physiological studies measuring stress response during repetition.

### 4. Quantitative
- Prevalence: what % of people report feeling stressed/irritated when repeating themselves?
- Effect sizes: how much does repetition increase cortisol, heart rate, or self-reported stress?
- Frequency data: how often do people repeat themselves daily (2025 UK study: 6 in 10)?
- Correlation between repetition stress and burnout, relationship satisfaction, workplace productivity.

### 5. Stakeholders
- The person repeating (feeling unheard, disrespected, cognitively drained)
- The listener who needs repetition (hearing loss, attention deficit, language barrier)
- Customer service agents and help-desk workers (chronic repetition stress)
- Teachers, parents, caregivers (high-repetition roles)
- Neurodivergent individuals (both sides of the repetition exchange)
- Healthcare providers (elderly care, dementia care)

### 6. Competing approaches
- Evolutionary psychology: repetition as status threat (being unheard = lower rank)
- Cognitive psychology: repetition as cognitive load / working memory friction
- Social psychology: repetition as respect violation / social norm breach
- Trauma-informed: over-explaining as protective fawn response from childhood invalidation
- Behavioral: repetition as learned habit of self-reassurance or control-seeking
- Neuropsychology: specific brain circuit dysregulation

### 7. Criticisms
- Is the stress of repetition overstated or pathologized unnecessarily?
- Potential conflation of "repeating myself" with "over-explaining" — are these distinct?
- Does cultural context moderate the stress response (collectivist vs individualist)?
- Sampling bias: most studies on WEIRD populations; repetition norms differ globally.

### 8. Contrarian
- Could repetition actually be beneficial or bonding in some contexts (rituals, teaching, parenting)?
- Are people who don't mind repeating themselves psychologically healthier in some ways?
- Does the stress of repetition reflect an ego problem (impatience, lack of empathy) rather than a legitimate psychological mechanism?
- Is modern society (digital communication, short attention spans) making this worse — or revealing an existing dynamic?

### 9. Future
- How will AI communication (chatbots, voice assistants that forget context) affect repetition stress?
- Will workplace trends (async communication, documentation culture) reduce or increase repetition burden?
- Potential interventions: communication training, organizational policy, therapeutic approaches.
- Generational shifts: do younger generations (raised on digital) have different repetition tolerance?

### 10. Regulatory / Institutional
- Workplace policies around communication load and repetition (customer service, healthcare).
- ADA and accommodation for hearing, attention, or cognitive disabilities.
- Call center industry standards for "repeat rate" and agent well-being.

### 11. Geographic
- Cross-cultural studies on repetition norms and stress responses.
- High-context vs low-context cultures: do they differ in repetition tolerance?
- Collectivist cultures: does repeating for group understanding feel less stressful?

### 12. Practical
- Evidence-based techniques to reduce stress when repeating yourself.
- Communication strategies for people who need others to repeat.
- How to distinguish between normal repetition and trauma-driven over-explaining.
- Workplace interventions: documentation, async communication, meeting norms.
- Therapeutic approaches: CBT, somatic, mindfulness for repetition-related stress.

### 13. Public opinion / user sentiment
- Reddit, Quora, social media discussions on "I hate repeating myself" — themes and frequency.
- Survey data on what types of repetition people find most stressful (personal vs professional, simple vs complex).
- Anecdotal patterns: "Are you listening to me?" as a common trigger phrase.
