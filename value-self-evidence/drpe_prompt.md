# DRPE Prompt

## Original user query

"no need to try to make people feel/believe something has value. if it really has value, people would just see it. philosophy"

## Research Question

What are the strongest philosophical arguments for and against the thesis that genuine value is self-evident — that if something truly has value, any rational observer would naturally recognize it without needing persuasion, explanation, or marketing?

## Audience & Purpose

A 54-year-old academic philosopher (father) and his 9-year-old child. The report informs whether the "self-evident value" thesis is philosophically defensible, and what it implies about the necessity (or lack thereof) of persuasion, rhetoric, and marketing for genuinely valuable things. The father wants rigorous argument; the child needs to follow along.

## Scope

**In:**
1. Plato's Form of the Good as the archetype of self-evident value (Republic, Allegory of the Cave, divided line)
2. Aristotle's critique — the good is not universally recognized; disagreement is endemic
3. Meno's paradox — how can we recognize value without already knowing it?
4. Epistemic humility (Socrates, Kant, Hume, Polanyi, Kuhn) — the limits of human value-perception
5. Aesthetic realism vs. subjectivism — is beauty/value in the object or the eye?
6. Cognitive biases (confirmation bias, Mere-exposure, Dunning-Kruger, availability) and their distortion of value recognition
7. Neuroscience of value — subjective valuation, reward prediction error, and social influence on perceived value
8. The paradox of persuasion — if value were self-evident, persuasion would be unnecessary; but persuasion exists and works, suggesting value is not transparent

**Out:**
1. Specific marketing tactics or business strategy
2. Religious or theological arguments about divine goodness
3. Economic value theory (labor theory, marginal utility) as primary lens
4. Neuroaesthetics in excessive technical detail
5. Case studies of specific products or companies

## Dimensions

### 1. Technical / Conceptual
- What does "self-evident" mean across different philosophical frameworks (foundationalism vs coherentism vs infinitism)?
- Is value perception analogous to color perception (secondary quality) or shape perception (primary quality)?
- Can value be a "response-dependent" property (disposition to elicit certain reactions in ideal observers)?
- The distinction between intrinsic, instrumental, and final value — which could plausibly be self-evident?

### 2. Historical
- Plato's Form of the Good as the source of the self-evidence view — the sun analogy (Republic 507b-509c)
- Aristotle's rejection — "the good is not something common and universal" (Nicomachean Ethics I.6)
- The Stoic concept of oikeiosis — natural affinity for recognizing what is appropriate
- Hume's sentiment-based value theory — "morality is more properly felt than judg'd of"
- Kant's distinction between the good will (self-evidently good) and everything else
- Nietzsche's critique — value is not discovered but created; the strong impose their valuations
- Moore's "good is a simple, non-natural property" and the open-question argument (Principia Ethica)
- Mackie's error theory — people think they perceive objective values, but they're wrong

### 3. State-of-Art / Contemporary
- Contemporary moral perception literature (Audi, Tappolet, McBrayer) — can we literally perceive value?
- The Cornell realists (Boyd, Sturgeon, Brink) — moral properties are natural and detectable
- Christine Korsgaard's constitutivism — value stems from the structure of agency, not perception
- Debates on "thick concepts" (Williams, Dancy, Väyrynen) — concepts like "cruel" mix description and evaluation
- Experimental philosophy data on folk intuitions about value objectivity
- The meta-problem of disagreement — if value were self-evident, why such persistent disagreement?

### 4. Quantitative / Empirical
- Implicit Association Test data on unconscious value judgments (Banaji & Greenwald, Blindspot)
- Kahneman & Tversky's framing effects — how presentation changes what people value
- The endowment effect — people value what they own more than what they don't
- Asch conformity experiments — social pressure overrides apparent value judgments
- Milgram — authority overrides apparently self-evident moral value (not harming others)
- Marshmallow test and delay discounting — people systematically undervalue future goods

### 5. Stakeholders
- Philosophers (who argue about value for a living — counterexample to self-evidence)
- Marketers and advertisers (their industry depends on value NOT being self-evident)
- Educators (transmitting value recognition — art appreciation, ethics education)
- Parents (teaching children to recognize value they don't yet see)
- Consumers (making value judgments under uncertainty)

### 6. Competing Approaches
- Value realism (value is objective, mind-independent) — strongest support for self-evidence
- Value subjectivism (value is projected by the mind) — strongest critique
- Value constructivism (value emerges from rational procedures) — middle ground
- Error theory (value judgments are systematically false) — the self-evidence intuition is an illusion
- Expressivism (value claims express attitudes, not beliefs) — no facts to be self-evident about
- Response-dependent accounts (value is disposition to elicit response in ideal observers under ideal conditions)

### 7. Criticisms
- The argument from disagreement: if value were self-evident, we'd expect convergence, not the deep moral and aesthetic pluralism we actually observe
- The argument from cognitive bias: humans are systematically unreliable detectors of value
- The argument from development: children don't naturally recognize value; it must be taught
- The argument from culture: what is valued varies dramatically across time and place
- The argument from ignorance: even the wisest disagree about what has value (Plato's Republic is a 300-page argument about what justice is)

### 8. Contrarian
- Perhaps value IS self-evident, but humans are systematically corrupted or ignorant (Plato's cave)
- Perhaps the very difficulty of recognizing value is evidence of its depth (great art is complex)
- Perhaps self-evidence comes in degrees; elementary values (pleasure is good, pain is bad) are self-evident, higher values are not
- The "eye of the beholder" objection proves too much — if value is entirely subjective, why do we argue about it at all?
- The appeal to ideal observers (Hume's "true judges," Smith's "impartial spectator," Rawls's "original position") — under the right conditions, value is indeed self-evident

### 9. Future
- AI value alignment — if human value recognition is unreliable, can we build machines that recognize value better?
- Neuroenhancement — could cognition-enhancing drugs or brain-computer interfaces make value more transparent?
- The future of marketing in a world where people believe they can "just see" value
- Moral progress — if value is self-evident, how do we explain moral progress (e.g., abolition of slavery)?

### 10. Regulatory / Political
- First Amendment implications — if value is self-evident, is persuasion a form of deception?
- Consumer protection law — if people can't reliably detect value, regulation is needed
- Education policy — should we teach value recognition, or just let people see for themselves?
- Advertising regulation — if value is self-evident, advertising is wasteful; if not, it's informative

### 11. Geographic / Cultural
- Western vs. East Asian conceptions of value recognition (Confucian role-based ethics vs. Platonic Forms)
- The Western tradition's emphasis on "seeing" (theoria, contemplation) vs. other epistemic metaphors
- Indigenous knowledge systems and their relationship to value perception
- How globalization reveals (or fails to reveal) cross-cultural value

### 12. Practical
- If you believe value is self-evident, you'd stop marketing, stop explaining, stop teaching, stop persuading — and you'd likely fail
- The practical contradiction: people who claim value is self-evident still argue for their position (the performative contradiction)
- Everyday decision-making under uncertainty about value — we use proxies (reputation, expert opinion, price) precisely because value is NOT transparent
- The "just build a great product" startup philosophy as a modern version of the self-evident value thesis

### 13. Public Opinion / User Sentiment
- Reddit discussions on "/r/philosophy" about whether "good art" is obvious or requires education
- Hacker News threads on "build it and they will come" vs. "you need marketing"
- User reviews of products people initially dismissed but later came to value
- Social media sentiment on whether "if it's good, it doesn't need advertising"
- Philosophical communities on whether moral truths are obvious to all rational beings
