# Phase 1: SCOPE — Research Framing

## Research Question
What are the strongest philosophical arguments for and against the thesis that genuine value is self-evident — that if something truly has value, any rational observer would naturally recognize it without needing persuasion, explanation, or marketing?

## Core Components Decomposed
1. **What does "self-evident" mean in philosophical contexts?** — Epistemic immediacy vs. inferential justification
2. **What does "value" encompass?** — Moral, aesthetic, epistemic, practical, intrinsic, instrumental, final
3. **Who is the "rational observer"?** — Ideal observer theory, ordinary human cognition, corrupted/uncorrupted perception
4. **What counts as "recognizing" value?** — Cognitive belief, emotional response, behavioral disposition, perceptual experience
5. **What is the alternative?** — If value is not self-evident, how is it known? (constructivism, subjectivism, error theory, expressivism)

## Stakeholder Perspectives
- **Academic philosophers** — professional disagreement about value is itself evidence against self-evidence
- **Marketers/advertisers** — their industry assumes value requires communication
- **Educators** — teaching value recognition (art, ethics) suggests it's not innate
- **Parents** — raising children requires explaining why things matter
- **Consumers** — daily value judgments under uncertainty
- **9-year-old child** — the test case: do children naturally recognize value?

## Scope Boundaries

### In Scope
1. Plato's Form of the Good — the archetype of self-evident value
2. Aristotle's critique — disagreement as evidence against universality
3. Meno's paradox — recognition presupposes knowledge
4. Epistemic humility traditions (Socrates, Kant, Hume, Polanyi, Kuhn)
5. Aesthetic realism vs. subjectivism
6. Cognitive biases distorting value recognition
7. Neuroscience of value (reward prediction error, social influence)
8. The paradox of persuasion
9. Ideal observer theory (Hume, Smith, Rawls, Firth)
10. Contemporary moral perception literature (Audi, Tappolet, McBrayer)
11. Thick concepts and evaluative perception
12. Experimental philosophy on value objectivity

### Out of Scope
1. Specific marketing tactics or business strategy
2. Religious or theological arguments about divine goodness
3. Economic value theory (labor theory, marginal utility) as primary lens
4. Neuroaesthetics in excessive technical detail
5. Case studies of specific products or companies
6. Purely theological meta-ethics

## Success Criteria
- 270+ sources minimum across 4+ source types
- Equally strong treatment of pro and con arguments
- Inline [imagine: ...] explanations for every hard concept
- Evidence class labels on every claim
- Executive summary >= 1000 words
- Weakest Evidence self-audit section
- All source types: academic, experimental psychology, user-reported, practitioner, historical primary texts

## Key Assumptions to Validate
1. That the "self-evident value" thesis is actually defended by serious philosophers (not just a straw man)
2. That disagreement about value is genuine and deep (not merely apparent)
3. That cognitive biases systematically distort value perception
4. That persuasion exists and works (paradox of persuasion)
5. That children and cultural outsiders fail to recognize certain values
