# Fastest Evidence-Based Methods to Increase Short-Term and Working Memory

## A Comprehensive, Evidence-Ranked Guide by Speed of Effect

**Date:** June 17, 2026

## Executive Summary

Working memory (WM) — the ability to hold and manipulate information over seconds [imagine: a mental notepad where you jot down a phone number before dialing, but also rearrange the digits in your head] — is central to learning, reasoning, and everyday function [1]. Declining WM is the #1 cognitive complaint of adults over 50 [2]. This report synthesizes 238 sources to answer a single practical question: what works fastest?

The evidence reveals a clear hierarchy by speed of effect. **Instant interventions** (minutes to hours): acute aerobic exercise boosts WM performance in 5-30 minutes via increased BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor — a protein that helps brain cells grow and connect) and cerebral blood flow [3,4]; a single 20-minute bout of moderate-intensity cycling improves WM accuracy by 0.3-0.5 standard deviations [5,6]. Caffeine (100mg) combined with L-theanine (200mg) produces measurable gains in attention-switching and working memory within 60 minutes [7,8]. Chunking — grouping information into meaningful units — and the method of loci (memory palace) produce near-immediate recall improvements with large effect sizes [9,10].

**Rapid interventions** (days to 2 weeks): sleep optimization is the single most impactful behavior — one night of sleep deprivation reduces WM performance 20-30%, while recovery sleep (including brief daytime naps restoring ~50% of cognitive decline) reverses this [11,12,13]. Eliminating multitasking and reducing cognitive load produces measurable gains within days [14].

**Medium-term interventions** (2-8 weeks): dual n-back training (20-25 min/day, 4-5 days/week) yields reliable WM improvements with SMD 0.18-0.37 [15,16], though far-transfer to fluid intelligence remains debated [17,18,19]. Mindfulness meditation (2 weeks, 4 sessions) improves WM capacity and GRE performance by reducing mind-wandering [20,21]. Bacopa monnieri (300mg/day) shows cognitive improvements as early as Day 14, with full effects at 12 weeks [22,23]. Magnesium L-threonate (MgT, 2g/day) reduced modeled cognitive age by 7.5 years in 6 weeks [24,25].

**Longer-term interventions** (8+ weeks): aerobic exercise (3x/week, 45-60 min) increases hippocampal volume 2% over 12 months [2,26]. Brain Endurance Training (BET) — combining cognitive tasks with exercise — produces superior gains to exercise alone [27,28]. Cistanche+Ginkgo combination enhances memory via cortico-cerebellar reorganization at 12 weeks [29].

The fastest protocol combining multiple mechanisms: **20-min aerobic exercise → 15-min dual n-back → caffeine+L-theanine → prioritized sleep**. This stack targets BDNF upregulation, cognitive load adaptation, neurotransmitter optimization, and consolidation respectively, and the evidence suggests synergistic benefits [27,30,31]. [imagine: like exercising different muscles — each intervention trains a different part of your memory system, so combining them works better than any single one.]

## 1. Introduction

### Scope and Methodology

This report investigates evidence-based methods to increase short-term memory (STM) and working memory (WM) capacity, ranked by speed of effect. The analysis covers interventions operating from minutes (acute exercise, caffeine, chunking) through hours (naps, focused attention), days (sleep optimization, cognitive load reduction), weeks (cognitive training, supplements, mindfulness), and months (aerobic exercise, neuromodulation).

The research employed a multi-level Breadth-First Search (BFS) expansion methodology across 12 dimensions of investigation, yielding 238 unique sources across 3 BFS levels. Sources include 139 academic papers (meta-analyses, RCTs, systematic reviews, neuroimaging studies), industry reports, government regulatory documents, expert commentary, and contrarian/critical analyses.

**For the 9-year-old reader:** Every time we use a technical term, we will immediately explain it in brackets like [imagine: this is the simple meaning]. The report is written for your parent's academic level, but the bracketed parts are just for you.

### Key Terms Defined

- **Short-term memory (STM):** The ability to hold a small amount of information for seconds [imagine: a sticky note in your brain that holds 5-9 items, like a phone number you just looked up] [1].
- **Working memory (WM):** STM plus the ability to manipulate that information [imagine: not just holding a phone number, but also reversing the digits or adding them up] [32].
- **BDNF (Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor):** A protein that helps brain cells grow and form new connections [imagine: fertilizer for brain cells] [33].
- **Neurogenesis:** The process of creating new neurons [imagine: your brain growing new brain cells] [34].
- **Near transfer:** Improvement on tasks similar to what you practiced [imagine: getting better at the exact game you practiced] [17].
- **Far transfer:** Improvement on tasks different from what you practiced [imagine: a memory game helping you do better in math class] [17,18].
- **Effect size (Cohen's d / Hedges' g):** How big an improvement is. d=0.2 is small, d=0.5 is medium, d=0.8+ is large [imagine: small = barely noticeable; large = obvious to anyone] [35].

## 2. Main Analysis

### Finding 1: Instant Interventions (Minutes to Hours)

**Acute Exercise.** A single bout of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise (20-30 min cycling or brisk walking at 60-75% max heart rate) produces immediate improvements in WM performance [3,4,5]. The mechanism involves an 11-20% increase in serum BDNF, which enhances synaptic plasticity and prefrontal cortex efficiency [33,36]. Effect sizes range from d=0.30 to d=0.55 for WM tasks performed immediately after exercise [6,37]. [imagine: just 20 minutes of brisk walking makes your brain work better right away — like warming up before a race.]

The timing matters: moderate-intensity exercise during encoding and retrieval phases produces state-dependent memory benefits [38]. Vigorous exercise (85%+ max heart rate) shows different effects — improving memory when performed *before* encoding rather than during [39]. A 2025 network meta-analysis found that cognitively engaging exercise (requiring decision-making) produced 2x the WM benefit of simple aerobic exercise [30,40].

**Caffeine + L-theanine.** Caffeine (100-160mg, about 1-2 cups of coffee) combined with L-theanine (200mg, the amino acid in green tea) in a 1:2 ratio produces synergistic cognitive enhancement within 60 minutes [7,8]. This stack improves attention-switching accuracy by 15-20%, reduces mind-wandering, and increases WM task performance [41,42,43]. [imagine: caffeine wakes you up, L-theanine calms the jitters — together they give you focused, calm energy.]

The systematic review by Kaur et al. (2021) confirmed that this combination outperforms either substance alone on measures of attention, reaction time, and cognitive performance [44]. L-theanine increases alpha brain waves (associated with relaxed alertness) while caffeine blocks adenosine (the chemical that makes you sleepy) [42].

**Chunking and the Method of Loci.** Chunking — grouping individual items into meaningful units — is the fastest strategy-based intervention [9]. [imagine: instead of remembering 9 separate numbers 1-7-7-6-2-0-0-5-1, you remember three chunks: 1776 (a year), 2005 (another year), 1 (the year America became independent).] The effect is immediate and large: chunking can boost recall from the classic 7±2 items to 15-20 items [9,45].

The method of loci (memory palace) — mentally placing items along a familiar spatial route — produces the largest effect sizes of any memory strategy [10,46]. A 2025 meta-analysis found Hedges' g = 1.2-1.8 for immediate serial recall [47]. A landmark 2017 study showed ordinary people more than doubled word recall after 6 weeks of 30-min daily training, with brain connectivity changes persisting 4 months [48]. [imagine: you walk through your house in your mind, putting each thing to remember in a different room — your brain is great at remembering places, so it works really well.]

### Finding 2: Rapid Behavioral Interventions (Days to 2 Weeks)

**Sleep Optimization.** Sleep is the single most impactful behavioral intervention for WM. One night of total sleep deprivation reduces WM performance 20-30% [11,49]. Partial sleep restriction (4-6 hours) over 5 nights produces cumulative degradation of attention, WM, and executive function — and these effects are not fully reversed by 2 nights of recovery sleep [50,51]. [imagine: your brain needs sleep like your phone needs charging — without it, nothing works properly.]

A systematic meta-analysis of 39 studies (n=1,234) found that restricting sleep to 3-6.5 hours (vs. 7-11 hours) negatively affects memory formation with Hedges' g = 0.29 [52]. Critically, restricting slow-wave sleep impairs memory more than restricting other stages [52].

Daytime naps are highly effective: a 2025 fMRI study found that brief naps restore nearly 50% of the WM decline caused by sleep deprivation, with reactivation of task-positive brain networks in the cerebellum and thalamus, and enhanced default mode network suppression [13,53]. [imagine: a 20-minute nap is like hitting the reset button for your brain.]

**Optimal sleep protocol for WM:**
- 7-9 hours nightly, consistent schedule [54]
- 20-30 min power naps (or 90-min full-cycle naps) when needed [13,53]
- Caffeine cutoff 8+ hours before bedtime [55]

**Cognitive Load Reduction and Single-Tasking.** Reducing cognitive load — the amount of mental effort being used in your working memory — produces immediate WM improvements [14]. Multitasking degrades WM performance by 30-50% compared to single-tasking [56,57]. [imagine: your mental notepad can only hold so much — every extra thing you think about takes up space. Doing one thing at a time gives you all your notepad space for that one thing.] Simply closing unnecessary browser tabs, silencing notifications, and working in focused blocks (Pomodoro technique: 25 minutes work, 5 minutes break) can improve WM-available capacity by 20-30% [14,58].

**Stress Reduction.** Acute stress elevates cortisol, which impairs prefrontal cortex function and WM retrieval [59,60]. A single session of deep breathing (5 min, 4-7-8 breathing pattern) reduces cortisol 15-25% and improves WM performance [61]. [imagine: stress is like static noise in your brain — breathing exercises turn down the volume.]

### Finding 3: Cognitive Training (Weeks)

**Dual N-Back Training.** Dual n-back — a task where you must remember both visual and auditory items from N steps back in a sequence — is the most studied cognitive training paradigm [15,16]. [imagine: someone shows you colored squares and says letters at the same time — you have to say whether each square and letter matches what you saw/heard 2 or 3 turns ago. It's like juggling two balls in your mind.]

Protocol: 20-30 minutes/day, 4-5 days/week, minimum 4 weeks [62]. Training produces dose-dependent improvements in the trained task — longer training yields higher N-levels achieved [63].

Effect sizes for near transfer (improvements on similar WM tasks): SMD 0.18-0.37 [15,16]. Far transfer (to fluid intelligence, reasoning) remains controversial. The 2016 Melby-Lervåg meta-analysis of 87 studies found no convincing far-transfer to intelligence, verbal ability, reading comprehension, or arithmetic [17,18]. However, Au et al. (2015) reported small but significant improvements in fluid intelligence (g=0.24) [64].

A 2025 double-blind RCT by Zhong et al. found that while training gains increased with longer training, there was no near or far transfer to untrained tasks — even with 20 sessions over 4 weeks [63]. Individual differences (cognitive ability, motivation, beliefs about intelligence) did not moderate outcomes [63].

[imagine: n-back is like getting really good at a specific video game — you get better at that game, but it doesn't necessarily make you better at totally different things like math or reading.]

**Executive N-Back and Adaptive Training.** Newer paradigms — "executive n-back" that incorporates more complex rules — may produce broader transfer [65]. Adaptive training (where difficulty adjusts to performance) is essential — fixed-difficulty training shows minimal effects [16,62].

**Complex Span Training.** Training on operation span, reading span, and other complex span tasks produces near-transfer comparable to n-back [66]. The combination of n-back and complex span may be more effective than either alone [67]. Cogmed, the commercialized version of adaptive WM training, has mixed evidence — some studies showing WM gains, others finding no advantage over active controls [21,68].

### Finding 4: Physical Exercise (Weeks to Months)

**Aerobic Exercise.** Regular aerobic exercise (3x/week, 45-60 min at moderate intensity) produces the most robust long-term WM improvements [2,26]. The mechanisms are well-established: exercise increases serum BDNF by 11-20%, promoting hippocampal neurogenesis and synaptic plasticity [33,34]. Over 12 months, aerobic exercise increases hippocampal volume by 2% — effectively reversing age-related hippocampal atrophy by 1-2 years [2,26]. [imagine: exercise is like sending a delivery truck of brain fertilizer to your memory center every time you work out.]

A 2025 meta-analysis of 133 RCTs found that exercise causes moderate improvements in cognition and smaller improvements in memory and executive function, across all age groups [69]. Children and teens showed the largest memory benefits [69].

**HIIT vs. Moderate vs. Resistance.** High-Intensity Interval Training (HIIT) produces larger acute BDNF increases (30-50% above baseline) than moderate exercise [70,71]. However, moderate exercise may be more sustainable for chronic effects. Resistance training shows benefits for executive function but less consistent effects on WM specifically [72]. [imagine: HIIT is a sprint — gives a quick boost; moderate jogging is longer lasting — like the difference between a power drink and a healthy meal.]

**Cognitively Engaging Exercise.** Exercise that requires decision-making (soccer, basketball, dance, baduanjin qigong) produces 2x the WM benefit of simple aerobic exercise alone [30,40]. A 2025 meta-analysis of 17 RCTs (n=419) found a medium-to-large effect for cognitively engaging exercise on WM, with the combined effect double that of aerobic alone [30,40]. [imagine: your brain has to work harder when you're both moving and thinking — that extra mental work makes your memory stronger.]

**Baduanjin and Mind-Body Exercise.** Baduanjin — an 8-movement Chinese qigong practice combining slow movement, breathing, and focus — has shown particular promise for WM in older adults [73,74]. An RCT found that 12 weeks of Baduanjin improved executive function and WM in adults with mild cognitive impairment, with neuroimaging showing increased functional connectivity in frontoparietal networks [73,74].

### Finding 5: Mindfulness & Meditation (Weeks)

Mindfulness meditation — training attention to focus on the present moment without judgment — improves WM through reducing mind-wandering [20,21]. [imagine: your brain has a "default mode" where it wanders off thinking about yesterday or worrying about tomorrow. Mindfulness is like a gentle leash that brings it back to what you're doing right now.]

Mrazek et al. (2013) found that just 2 weeks of mindfulness training (4 sessions, 45 min each) improved WM capacity and GRE reading comprehension scores by 16%, mediated by reduced mind-wandering [20]. The effect was strongest among participants prone to distraction at baseline [20].

Jha et al. demonstrated that mindfulness training strengthens sustained attention and WM more effectively than relaxation training or positivity training [75,76]. A 2025 USC study found that 30 days of app-guided meditation (10-15 min daily) improved attention switching accuracy and speed [77]. [imagine: just 10 minutes a day of a meditation app for a month can help you focus better and remember more.]

The mechanisms involve reduced default mode network activity (the "mind-wandering network"), increased prefrontal cortex efficiency, and lower stress-related cortisol [59,78]. Effect sizes: d=0.4-0.6 for WM improvements after 2-8 weeks of practice [20,79].

**Optimal protocol:** 10-15 min daily focused attention meditation (e.g., following the breath), or 45 min sessions 2-3x/week [20,75].

### Finding 6: Supplements & Nootropics (Weeks)

**Bacopa monnieri (Brahmi).** An Ayurvedic herb with the strongest evidence base among cognitive supplements [22,23]. A 2024 double-blind RCT (n=80, 300mg/day Bacopa extract containing 90mg bacosides) found significant improvements in verbal STM, spatial STM, WM, and episodic memory at 12 weeks [22]. Cognitive improvements were detectable as early as Day 14 [22]. A meta-analysis of 9 RCTs (n=437) confirmed improvements in cognitive processing speed at 12 weeks [23,80]. [imagine: Bacopa is like a memory tonic used in India for thousands of years — modern science shows it actually works.]

Mechanisms include BDNF upregulation, NMDA receptor modulation, enhanced cerebral blood flow, and acetylcholinesterase inhibition [80,81]. The effect size for memory is moderate: d=0.3-0.5 [22].

**Magnesium L-threonate (Magtein/MgT).** A form of magnesium specifically designed to cross the blood-brain barrier [24,25]. A 2026 double-blind RCT (n=106, 2g MgT/day) found that MgT supplementation improved cognitive performance — corresponding to a 7.5-year reduction in modeled cognitive age after just 6 weeks [24]. [imagine: this magnesium is special because it actually gets into your brain — most magnesium doesn't — and it makes brain cells communicate better.]

Mechanisms: MgT increases synaptic density and plasticity in the hippocampus, modulates NMDA receptors, and enhances mitochondrial function in brain cells [82,83]. The 2026 study is one of the most promising supplement RCTs in recent years, though replication in larger samples is needed [24].

**Lion's Mane Mushroom (Hericium erinaceus).** Promotes nerve growth factor (NGF) synthesis in preclinical models [84,85]. Clinical evidence is mixed: one RCT (n=30, MCI patients) found improved cognitive test performance at 16 weeks, but effects faded after discontinuation [86]. Another RCT (n=31, healthy adults over 50) found improvement on only 1 of 3 cognitive tests [87]. Evidence is insufficient for strong recommendations [88]. [imagine: Lion's Mane is a mushroom that might help brain cells grow, but the science isn't fully settled yet — it's promising but not proven.]

**Cistanche tubulosa + Ginkgo biloba.** A combination shown to enhance memory via cortico-cerebellar reorganization [29,89]. A 2026 RCT (n=100, 90 days) found improvements on the Wechsler Memory Scale and Montreal Cognitive Assessment [29]. The effect appears synergistic — each herb augments the other's bioavailability and mechanisms [89]. [imagine: two herbs that work better together than either alone — like peanut butter and jelly.]

**Fish Oil (DHA/EPA).** Omega-3 fatty acids are essential for brain structure and function. Supplementation improves WM in individuals with low baseline omega-3 levels, but effects are small (d=0.1-0.2) in healthy adults [90]. The evidence is strongest for preventing age-related decline rather than acute enhancement [90].

**Ketone Bodies (Exogenous Ketones).** A 2026 systematic review found emerging evidence that ketone bodies may improve cognitive function in healthy adults, with stronger effects in populations with metabolic dysfunction [91,92]. A single dose of ketone monoester can improve cognitive processing speed in older adults with prediabetes [93]. The mechanism involves providing an alternative brain fuel that bypasses impaired glucose metabolism [91]. [imagine: ketones are like a backup fuel source for your brain — when it can't use sugar well (like with age or diabetes), ketones give it energy to keep working.]

**Caffeine + L-theanine.** (See Finding 1 for acute effects.) Chronic use maintains baseline cognitive performance but tolerance develops, reducing net benefit [94]. Cycling (2 weeks on, 1 week off) may preserve sensitivity [94].

**Summary of supplement evidence quality:**

| Supplement | Evidence Strength | Speed of Effect | Effect Size | Recommendation |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| Caffeine + L-theanine | Strong | Minutes | Medium | Use acutely before cognitive tasks |
| Bacopa monnieri | Strong | 2-12 weeks | Medium | Good for sustained use |
| MgT (Magtein) | Moderate | 6 weeks | Medium-Large | Promising, needs replication |
| Cistanche+Ginkgo | Moderate | 12 weeks | Medium | Promising combo |
| Lion's Mane | Weak | 16 weeks | Small | Insufficient evidence |
| Fish Oil | Moderate | 6-12 weeks | Small | Better for prevention |
| Exogenous Ketones | Emerging | Minutes-hours | Unknown | Needs more research |

### Finding 7: Neuromodulation — tACS and Neurofeedback

**Transcranial Alternating Current Stimulation (tACS).** tACS applies gentle electrical currents to the scalp at specific frequencies to synchronize brain oscillations [95]. [imagine: your brain has natural rhythms like music beats — tACS is like a conductor making sure different parts of your brain play in time with each other.]

Theta-frequency (4-8 Hz) tACS over prefrontal cortex improves WM by enhancing theta-gamma coupling — the coordination between slower and faster brain rhythms that is essential for cognitive control [96,97]. A 2024 study found that real-time phase-tuned tACS improved WM accuracy with Cohen's d = 0.976 — a very large effect [97]. [imagine: this is a brain stimulation technique so precise it adjusts to your brain's live rhythms, like noise-canceling headphones that adapt to the sound around you.]

Gamma-frequency (40 Hz) tACS has shown benefit in MCI and Alzheimer's patients, with 25 min of stimulation improving episodic memory [98,99]. A 2025 RCT found that 5 sessions of bifrontal tACS produced WM improvements that persisted at 4-week follow-up [100].

**Theta Neurofeedback.** Training individuals to increase theta (4-8 Hz) power through neurofeedback — where you learn to control your brain waves by watching them on a screen — improves WM in several studies [101,102]. Effect sizes are moderate (d=0.4-0.6) but require 10-20 sessions [101].

**Limitations:** tACS and neurofeedback devices are not FDA-approved for cognitive enhancement. Consumer devices (e.g., Muse, Halo) have limited regulation and weaker evidence [103]. The 2016 FTC action against Lumosity established precedent that cognitive benefit claims require substantial evidence [104]. [imagine: be careful with brain stimulation gadgets you can buy online — they may not work as well as advertised, and the government has fined companies that made false claims.]

### Finding 8: Combinatorial & Multimodal Protocols

The most effective approach combines interventions targeting different mechanisms [27,28,30]. **Brain Endurance Training (BET)** — performing cognitive tasks (e.g., 20-min Stroop or n-back) before or during exercise — produces superior cognitive gains to exercise or cognitive training alone [27,28]. A 2025 RCT in older adults found BET improved WM 7.8% when fatigued vs. 3.7% for exercise alone [27]. [imagine: BET is like doing math problems while running on a treadmill — your brain learns to work hard even when you're tired.]

**The "Fastest Gains" Stack** (based on converging evidence across multiple studies):
1. **Morning:** 20-min moderate exercise (BDNF boost) [3,4]
2. **Post-exercise:** Caffeine (100mg) + L-theanine (200mg) for focused cognitive work [7,8]
3. **Daily cognitive training:** 15-min adaptive dual n-back or complex span [62,63]
4. **Optimized sleep:** 7-9 hours + strategic 20-min naps when fatigued [11,52,53]
5. **Background supplements:** Bacopa (300mg/day) + MgT (2g/day) for sustained benefit [22,24]

**Why stacking works:** Each intervention targets a different mechanism — exercise increases BDNF and blood flow, caffeine+L-theanine optimizes neurotransmitter levels, cognitive training drives synaptic adaptation, sleep consolidates gains, and supplements provide nutritional support [27,30,31]. [imagine: think of your memory like a garden. Exercise is watering, supplements are fertilizer, sleep is letting the plants rest, cognitive training is pruning, and caffeine+L-theanine is sunlight. Do them all and the garden thrives.]

## 3. Synthesis & Insights

**The speed-benefit tradeoff is the critical decision framework.** Instant interventions (exercise, caffeine, chunking) produce reliable but transient effects lasting hours. Medium-term interventions (n-back, mindfulness, Bacopa) produce more durable but modest gains requiring weeks of commitment. The combination strategy — using acute tools for immediate needs while building chronic capacity through sustained training — emerges as the evidence-supported optimal approach.

**Sleep optimization is the highest-leverage intervention.** No other intervention produces the simultaneous breadth and magnitude of effect on WM. A single poor night reduces WM 20-30% [11,49] — wiping out weeks of gains from other interventions. Conversely, consistent sleep optimization provides a foundation that amplifies all other interventions [54].

**The far-transfer debate is partially resolved.** After 20 years of research, the consensus (2025-2026) is that cognitive training produces reliable near-transfer but far-transfer is modest at best and heavily dependent on training design [63,105]. This is not a null finding — it tells us to manage expectations: WM training improves WM, but it is not a shortcut to general intelligence. [imagine: you can absolutely get better at remembering things, but it's not a magic pill that makes you smarter at everything.]

**Commercial brain training claims require scrutiny.** The Lumosity $2M FTC settlement (2016) established precedent that cognitive improvement claims require substantiation [104]. Many commercial programs (Cogmed, BrainHQ, Peak) have effect sizes that decline when independent researchers (versus company-affiliated) conduct trials [21,68]. Consumers should prioritize free/verified protocols (dual n-back apps, open-source cognitive tasks) over paid subscriptions.

**The supplement industry has an evidence problem.** Of the supplements reviewed, only caffeine+L-theanine and Bacopa monnieri have strong, consistent evidence. MgT is promising but based on one company-sponsored trial [24]. Lion's Mane, Ginkgo, fish oil, and most nootropic stacks have mixed or weak evidence. Consumers should approach supplement claims with healthy skepticism and prioritize interventions with independent replication.

## 4. Limitations & Caveats

**Publication bias.** The working memory training literature is affected by publication bias — studies with null results are less likely to be published [17,18]. When corrected for publication bias, far-transfer effect sizes approach zero [17]. The supplement literature has similar issues: industry-funded trials are more likely to report positive results [22,24].

**Small effect sizes.** Many "significant" results have small effect sizes (d=0.2-0.3). A quarter standard deviation improvement in WM from 4 weeks of n-back training may not be subjectively noticeable [16,63]. For context: the WM decline from a single night of poor sleep (d=0.6-0.9) is 2-3x larger than the gain from 4 weeks of training [11].

**Lack of standardization.** WM is measured with dozens of different tasks — digit span, operation span, n-back, Corsi blocks, Sternberg task, etc. — which correlate only moderately (r=0.3-0.5) [106]. An intervention that improves one WM measure may not improve another.

**Small samples.** Many supplement RCTs have fewer than 50 participants per arm [22,84]. The largest MgT trial had 53 per group [24]. Small samples inflate effect sizes and reduce replicability.

**Age-specific effects.** Most WM training studies use young adults (age 18-30) [15,16,63]. The effects in older adults (50+) may differ substantially. MgT showed a 7.5-year brain age reduction but in adults aged 18-45 [24] — effects in 54-year-olds may differ. Bacopa studies in older adults have been more mixed than in younger adults [23].

**Contradictory evidence.** The WM training field is defined by contradiction: Jaeggi (2008) found far-transfer to fluid intelligence [107]; Melby-Lervåg (2016) found none [17]; Zhong (2025) found neither near nor far transfer in a well-controlled trial [63]. Each camp has methodological critiques of the other. The practical resolution: if far-transfer exists, it is small enough that individuals may not notice it.
