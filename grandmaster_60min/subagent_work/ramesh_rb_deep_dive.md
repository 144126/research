## 1. Executive Summary

GM Ramesh RB (Ramachandran Ramesh) is widely regarded as the most successful scholastic chess coach in the world. Through his Chennai-based academy Chess Gurukul (founded 2008), he has trained Praggnanandhaa, Gukesh Dommaraju, Vaishali, Arjun Erigaisi, and dozens of other Grandmasters. As of 2026, four of the world's top seven juniors have been Ramesh's students. This is not coincidence — it is the result of a deliberate, systematic coaching philosophy that he has refined over two decades.

Ramesh's core insight is that **teaching is NOT transferring information**. Most chess instruction — books, videos, lectures — tells the student what to do. Ramesh argues this is fundamentally ineffective because knowing is not doing. Instead, he designs positions and uses Socratic questioning to force students to discover concepts themselves. His method is built on five pillars:

1. **Active discovery over passive instruction** — Students must apply principles immediately, make mistakes, and identify those mistakes themselves through guided questioning.
2. **Process over outcome** — Focus on effort and learning, not results and ratings.
3. **Thinking process refinement** — Chess decisions are the product of a thinking process; refine the process, and the moves take care of themselves.
4. **No engine crutch** — Students train without engines until 2200+ FIDE, developing their own analytical skills.
5. **Engineered positions** — Ramesh creates positions at 5 levels (1400 to GM) that force specific learning outcomes, with students solving without moving pieces.

His two books — *Fundamental Chess: Logical Decision Making* (2013) and *Improve Your Chess Calculation* (2022, Chess.com Book of the Year) — along with his Chessable course and FritzTrainer, represent the only published codification of his methods. But the method itself is best understood through the extensive interviews he has given to New In Chess, ChessBase, Next Level Chess, and various podcasts.

## 2. Biography & Context

GM Ramesh RB was born in 1976 in Chennai, India. He was a strong player in his own right — British Champion in 2002, and a Grandmaster who represented India. His journey from player to coach began accidentally:

> *"Immediately after my success in '99, more players started approaching me. They were all around 2300 and above, all trying to become International Masters. Back then, we had only three Grandmasters in India, so no one was really trying to become a Grandmaster — we were all trying to get into the Indian team."* — Dina Belenkaya interview, January 2026

He started by working with players above 2300, then progressively lowered the level to test his effectiveness. He worked with 2000-rated players, then 1800, and eventually 1400. This descent taught him that **the same fundamental problems plague players at every level** — poor thinking habits, lack of curiosity about the opponent's resources, fear of losing, and passive learning.

In 2008, he founded Chess Gurukul with his wife, WGM Aarthie Ramaswamy. The name "Gurukul" is deliberate — it references the traditional Indian education system where students live with their teacher and learn through immersive experience, not just instruction.

> *"Chess Gurukul is the first chess academy which catered to all levels of players — from complete beginners to World championship contenders."* — ChessGurukul.com

His daily schedule reveals his intensity: waking at 3:45 AM for online sessions with 250+ students across the US, Europe, and Australia, followed by in-person classes at the academy from 5 AM to 7:30 PM with breaks for more online sessions.

Ramesh has served as coach of the Indian National Team for multiple Chess Olympiads (2012-2018, 2022), leading India-2 to a historic bronze medal at the 2022 Chennai Olympiad. In 2018, he was named FIDE Junior Trainer of the Year.

## 3. "Teaching is NOT Transferring" — The Core Philosophy

This is Ramesh's most frequently quoted principle and the foundation of everything he does. It emerges most powerfully in his interview with GM Noël Studer for Next Level Chess.

### The Exact Statement

> *"I will not show a game and demonstrate what is good or bad. Immediately, my students need to try to apply the theory in practice. When they come up with a wrong move, I will ask them: which principle are you breaking? So they will have to find out: 'Oh, I'm moving my same piece again.' I just ask the questions. I don't tell them 'you are moving the same piece twice.' Then it is just information. Teaching is NOT transferring information."* — Next Level Chess Part 2, 2022

### The Deeper Meaning

Ramesh draws a sharp distinction between **knowledge** (information you possess) and **skill** (the ability to act on that knowledge under pressure):

> *"Another key ingredient is to understand the difference between knowledge and skill. We acquire knowledge in many different ways: reading books, watching videos, or listening to a Coach. As I said before, knowing isn't doing. What is missing is the experience with that knowledge. Once we get experience acting on the learned knowledge, we form a skill."* — Next Level Chess Part 1, 2022

This is not merely pedagogical preference — it is grounded in a deep understanding of how the brain learns. Ramesh uses a powerful analogy:

> *"You can try to convince a child with a fear of dogs with loads of information and facts. But it won't work. Even if you tell this child that a dog doesn't eat you, the child will still run away when seeing a dog. The instincts of the child will kick in. You need to ask what the fears of the child are, and then take small steps with the child. This hand-holding process is what teaching is all about."* — Next Level Chess Part 2

### Why Most Chess Books Fail

Ramesh is unusually critical of traditional chess literature:

> *"Sadly, most books on Chess are just transferring information. They are written by (former) players, not Coaches. To them, many things come easily. Nobody had to convince Anand to develop pieces quickly. When people that are excelling in a field write a book, they write from their perspective: many things come very easily and are logical. Most authors will just write about different things you need to do. If you do these things, you will enjoy the benefits. But it isn't that easy."* — Next Level Chess Part 2

He identifies a structural problem: talented players make poor teachers because they cannot relate to the struggles of average students.

### The Hand-Holding Process

Ramesh describes teaching as a gradual, individualized process:

> *"So in order to help, I need to understand WHY this specific student is not making the list [of candidate moves] and then help them solve that problem. Most students won't tell you the reason, you need to dig deeper as a Coach and find it out. Nearly everything we do becomes a habit with time. So in chess training, I need to understand what habits a student has, how they can break them, and implement new, better ones."* — Next Level Chess Part 2

## 4. The Engineered-Position Discovery Method

This is the practical application of "teaching is not transferring." Ramesh does not explain concepts abstractly. He creates positions that force the student to confront a specific problem, and then guides them through the discovery process.

### How It Works

**Step 1: State the principle briefly.** For opening principles, for example, Ramesh lists what to do (develop quickly, castle, fight for center) and what not to do (early queen development, too many pawn moves).

**Step 2: Immediate application.** No demonstration games. No annotated examples. The student must immediately play positions that test the principle.

**Step 3: Socratic questioning.** When the student makes a mistake, Ramesh asks: "Which principle are you breaking?" The student must identify their own error. When the student makes the right move, Ramesh asks: "Which principle did you just follow?"

**Step 4: Personal experience.** The insight becomes the student's own discovery, not something they were told.

> *"Like this, it is their own personal experience. If they just hear 'you didn't develop quickly' it is just a piece of information. It doesn't make an impression on them or change them."*

### The 5-Level Calculation System

Ramesh has developed a structured system of engineered positions spanning 5 levels:

> *"Over the years I have gathered a lot of material on calculation. First I started with IMs and helped them get to GM Level. Then I have constantly lowered the level of students I can start working with. Now, I have 5 Levels to bring someone from roughly 1400 (FIDE) to a GM Level in Calculation in 2-3 years."*

Each level contains positions specifically selected to expose a particular thinking error. The student solves them in front of Ramesh, who observes their process:

> *"I let each student solve positions in front of me. Like this, I can understand what is going wrong in their thought process. Again, I always ask them what they think they did right or wrong. I help them identify their own mistakes. Like this, it is them explaining their personal experience."*

### The "No Moving Pieces" Rule

This is the most distinctive feature of Ramesh's training. Students must solve positions **without moving the pieces**, training visualization and forcing deep calculation:

> *"Do not move the pieces while solving or analysing a position. Try to find good defensive resources for your opponent as well. Finding the first few moves is not enough; try to find the complete solution wherever possible."* — Improve Your Chess Calculation, Introduction

In the Crete training camp with GM Noël Studer (2400+ players), Ramesh forced them to calculate a single position in their heads for over one hour: *"We could not move any pieces, nor did we see the initial position after a while. It was the first time I did something like this, and I faced many practical difficulties."*

### The Drawback Principle

One of Ramesh's most important contributions to chess thinking: **every move has a drawback**. In dynamic positions, instead of just finding "good moves," the student must actively look to exploit the immediate drawback of the opponent's last move. This is trained through engineered positions where the drawback is the key to the solution.

### CCTP Method

Ramesh adds **Pawn Breaks** to the standard Checks-Captures-Threats framework, creating CCTP (Checks, Captures, Threats, Pawn Breaks). He argues that many players miss tactical solutions because they ignore pawn breaks as forcing moves.

## 5. The "No Lectures, Only Positions" Approach

While Ramesh is ironically known among his students for his "advice and long lectures" (as he says in the Chess.com interview), his actual training sessions minimize lecturing and maximize active solving.

### What This Looks Like in Practice

> *"In training, I never show things. Let us say I want to teach opening principles to my students. I will tell them the things they need to do. And then I also tell them the things they should NOT do. Now when they have the theory, I want them to apply it immediately. I will not show a game and demonstrate what is good or bad."* — Next Level Chess Part 2

### The Swimming Analogy

Ramesh compares chess training to learning to swim: you cannot learn by watching videos of Olympic swimmers. You need to get in the water, struggle, and figure out the technique with guided feedback.

### The "Training Should Be Harder Than the Game" Principle

> *"That way we extend the limit. Only if we push our limits we can truly improve. Our limits are usually self-created in our own minds. We don't rise to the level of our expectations; we fall to the level of our training."* (quoting Archilochus)

This is why he uses difficult, engineered positions that force students beyond their comfort zone. Learning happens when training is **active** and **challenging**, not passive and comfortable.

### The Learning Check

> *"In training, the main question is: is the learning happening? As long as the learning is happening, the training makes sense. Most of the training is happening without learning. It is just an accumulation of information."*

## 6. Specific Methods Used With Praggnanandhaa and Gukesh

### Praggnanandhaa: Coach Since Age 7

Ramesh began working with Praggnanandhaa when he was 7.5 years old. His approach was shaped by Pragg's exceptional maturity and work ethic:

> *"When I met him he was around 7.5 years old. Already then in a chess sense he was mature. He knew he will become a strong player. And he knew he will not get anywhere without hard work. I did not have to convince him at all."*

**Visualization Training:** Ramesh trained Pragg's visualization extensively by having sessions where they discussed and analyzed positions verbally without moving pieces:

> *"We do a lot of sessions where we train without moving pieces on the board. We just discuss the moves verbally and we analyse positions. But we don't move any pieces. Most players train by moving pieces on the board, but when you do that your visualisation skills will not be so good. That's why Pragg is probably the best among all the other players in his generation when it comes to visualisation."*

**Independence Training:** By the time Pragg was competing at the highest level, Ramesh deliberately weaned him off dependence:

> *"I also tell Pragg to be very independent and that he should not be dependent on anyone for his chess preparations during major tournaments. In the last two years, we have worked a lot in this direction. Now, he's very independent and can take care of himself, he can take major decisions like which openings I should play, what should be my approach, how to manage his time. He's doing all this by himself largely. We keep in touch through WhatsApp."*

**Precision Tournament Preparation:** For Pragg's famous victory over Magnus Carlsen at the Airthings Masters (2022), Ramesh engineered a 10-day sleep schedule adjustment:

> *"We decided 10 days before the tournament, he started sleeping at 4 AM in the morning. He started his preparation at 10:30 PM in the night when the tournament would start, so that his mind and body adapted and was preparing till 3 AM in the morning. He was doing it for 10 days."*

Each day during the tournament, they would devise strategy for the four daily games: *"For all the four games, what should be our approach, what openings we should choose, and what is the opponent's strength and weakness."*

**Psychological Coaching:** Ramesh addressed Pragg's emotional and mental state directly, especially when Gukesh and other contemporaries surged ahead:

> *"There were things we discussed at the end of last year. Does it have a negative impact on you? Because Gukesh, rating wise, he went up. Pragg was 2740 in January. He had some ups and downs. 'So do others' performances have an impact on you?' These were some of the issues we discussed."*

He insists on radical honesty: *"If my student is not going to be frank with me, I won't be working with them. And I'm not going to be honest with my students, they should not be working with me. There should be some trust between the trainer and the player."*

**Comparative Assessment of Pragg's Chess:** In the 2026 Dina Belenkaya interview, Ramesh gave a remarkably candid assessment:

> *"Chess-wise, I would say his chess was much better than the other two [Gukesh and Arjun], but I feel in the last two or three years he has kind of molded himself in one particular way. His strengths in other areas are not that visible. Some of his main strengths are well-hidden now."*  
> *"He can actually play complicated positions well. But if given a choice between playing safe and choosing a risky continuation, he tends toward choosing the safer option. That is also probably because he has been playing an insane amount of tournaments in the last couple of years."*

### Gukesh: Minimal Hands-On, Maximum Independence

Ramesh's relationship with Gukesh was notably different — less intensive personal coaching, more of a strategic overview:

> *"In the case of Gukesh, I could not get to know Gukesh before the [Olympiad] event. Gukesh was working on his own throughout the event. He wanted his own privacy, which we all wanted to respect."*

**Engine-Free Development:** Gukesh became a Grandmaster at age 12 without using chess engines — directly following Ramesh's philosophy of developing natural analytical skill before relying on computer assistance. He didn't start using engines regularly until past 2540 FIDE.

**Assessment of Gukesh's Strengths:**

> *"His main strength is his insane self-belief. He just thinks he is too good, and that helps him. He can do dubious things and still get away with them. That gives him the courage to do risky or insane things, and even in difficult situations, he is still believing in himself."*

**Comparative with Arjun Erigaisi:**

> *"Arjun's style is more enterprising, and he can bounce back from bad performances. He can play a tournament badly, and in the next tournament, he can be a monster. In my view, he's kind of a complete player and manages his time much better than the other two."*

## 7. Handling Students of Different Levels

### The Progressive Descent

Ramesh's career shows a deliberate expansion downward in student level:

1. **Phase 1:** 2300+ players (IMs trying to become GMs)
2. **Phase 2:** 2000-2300 players
3. **Phase 3:** 1800-2000 players
4. **Phase 4:** 1400-1800 players (his current lowest level for the calculation system)

He explains: *"I started experimenting by working with players below 2,300 and so on and then I started slowly getting lower and lower like 2,000, 1800 and I was trying to test myself where I'm most effective."*

### The 5-Level Calculation System

Ramesh has formalized 5 levels covering 1400 FIDE to GM level in 2-3 years. Each level has:
- Engineered positions targeting specific thinking errors
- A clear gateway: when Ramesh is convinced the student has internalized the process, they advance
- Positions of increasing complexity that force the student to apply the same principles

### Group Training Across Levels

Unlike most academies, Chess Gurukul trains students in mixed groups:

> *"I always have my kids train in groups. And I try to make sure it's a good mix — there is no differentiation in terms of age or their ELO ratings. I want my Grandmasters to learn from the rest, and the youngsters to get inspired by the senior players."*

This is a deliberate philosophy: younger/lower-rated students learn from observing the thinking processes of stronger students, and stronger students reinforce their understanding by explaining.

### Readiness-Based Advancement

Ramesh does not advance students by time or curriculum completion, but by demonstrated readiness:

> *"Once I'm convinced the student understood the process, it is just a matter of doing the work. By solving enough positions in Level 1, they will go to Level 2. And so on. It is like you started a motorbike and now you should just accelerate, the speed will automatically improve. But to start the bike and learn how to ride the bike is the key."*

### Adapting to Adults vs. Children

Per the Chess Tournament Guide analysis: *"Yes, but with adjustments. Ramesh primarily trains young players, but the underlying method — active learning, no engines until you're strong, deep work over many topics, and not chasing every new course — applies to anyone. Adults often have to be more patient with themselves and accept that improvement is slower than for children."*

## 9. The Thinking Process Framework

Ramesh's central doctrine is that **chess is a thinking process, not a knowledge game**. His approach to coaching is to identify and refine patterns in the student's thinking.

> *"Chess decisions are the product of a particular way of thinking. If we can refine the thinking process, then the moves will also take care of themselves. So we focus a lot on the thinking process."* — Dina Belenkaya interview

### The Seven-Step Thinking Process (from Fundamental Chess)

In his first book, Ramesh breaks down the thinking process into seven steps:

1. Assessment of the position
2. Identifying the ideas and drawbacks of the opponent's move
3. Making a list of possible moves or ideas
4. Analyzing options efficiently and correctly
5. Finishing analysis with appropriate evaluation
6. Deciding on a move based on evaluation
7. Managing time on the clock throughout

### The "Simple to Complex to Simple" Evolution

In his Chessmood article (2020), Ramesh explains the growth arc of a chess player:

> *"We need to cultivate our analytical skills in a way we can search for truth in all its glory without letting our shortcomings get in the way. This quest for growing from simple to complex and finally the whole thing becoming simple again led man to come up with matchboxes, lighters, cooking gas, turbines, boilers, engines, etc. We evolved from simple to complex. Complexity is not something we should be afraid of. From complexity things eventually become simpler. Our current low competence level should not scare us from evolving from simple to complex."*

This maps onto his teaching: students start with simple principles, struggle with complex positions that challenge those principles, and eventually internalize the principles so deeply that the complex becomes simple again.

### Dynamic vs. Static Position Classification

Ramesh developed a practical position classification system described in *Improve Your Chess Calculation*:

**Dynamic positions:** Things change quickly, plenty of action, many factors constantly evolving. King safety, time, and piece quality matter more than material parity. Forcing moves dominate.

**Static positions:** Closed, quiet, calm. Fixed pawn structures. Forcing moves are not possible. General principles and long-term planning apply.

He argues that players must be able to handle both types, and that most errors come from applying static thinking to dynamic positions (or vice versa).

## 10. Engine Usage Philosophy

Ramesh is famously anti-engine for developing players, and his track record backs this up:

> *"Gukesh became a Grandmaster at age 12 without using engines and didn't start using one regularly until past 2540."*

His position:

> *"Engines aren't wrong, but they're a crutch that prevents improvers from developing their own analytical skills. He recommends solving puzzles, analyzing games, and thinking through positions yourself first. Once you've reached your conclusion, then check with the engine if you must. The wrong way: open the position and turn the engine on immediately. The right way: think for 20+ minutes, write your conclusion, then verify."*

He recommends almost no engine use until ~2200 FIDE, and minimal engine use even up to 2400-2500.

## 11. Psychological Dimensions

### Fear of Losing

Ramesh identifies fear as the single biggest obstacle to improvement:

> *"What is holding me back from sacrificing a pawn? It's my fear of losing. If I don't solve that issue, this guy is not going to learn to sacrifice pawns. If they learn to deal with the loss process better, they'll be confident to sacrifice pawns. This is my approach to chess training."*

### The Outsourcing Problem

Ramesh warns repeatedly against students outsourcing their learning:

> *"Many children think: 'Let the coach make me a better player. I don't have to do much work.' That's the general attitude of many young kids these days. They have to take ownership of their progress. They have to play the main role in their own success. Only then the other factors will be helpful. If they outsource everything to trainers, it's heartbreak after a few years."*

### Parents as Part of the System

Ramesh works extensively with parents, recognizing that the family environment shapes the child's relationship with chess:

> *"My suggestion is that the parents should also be taken into confidence by the coach. Parents should play their part correctly. Because when the coach teaches something and the parents teach something else, the child will be confused — he will have to choose between his parents or the coach. It's not a good situation for a child to be in."*

### Emotional Investment

From the ChessBase interview:

> *"Unless you are emotionally invested, it's tough to become a good coach. A coach has to go through both the happy and difficult moments the player goes through. He or she has to go through all the emotions, empathize with the players and guide them in the proper way. It's a very intense process."*

### The Role of Psychology

Ramesh increasingly emphasizes psychology:

> *"After working for a couple of decades as a trainer, I feel that the role of psychology has been drastically underestimated by trainers around the world. I want to focus more on that along with the technical aspects of the game. If something is preventing an individual from learning things, those issues need to be addressed."*

## 12. Synthesis: The Complete Ramesh Method

### The Pedagogical Core

1. **Principle first, then immediate application** — Brief explanation followed by immediate practice in engineered positions
2. **Socratic questioning always** — Never tell the student what they did wrong; ask them to identify it
3. **Personal experience over transferred information** — The insight must be the student's own discovery
4. **Process over outcome** — Focus on effort, learning, and the thinking process, not results or ratings
5. **Harder-than-game training** — Push limits in training so games feel easier

### The Technical Core

1. **5-level calculation system** — Progressively harder engineered positions from 1400 to GM
2. **No moving pieces** — Train visualization by solving entirely in the head
3. **CCTP method** — Checks, Captures, Threats, Pawn Breaks as the forcing-move hierarchy
4. **Drawback principle** — Every move has a downside; look for it
5. **Dynamic/Static classification** — Know which thinking mode to apply
6. **No engines until 2200+** — Develop natural analytical skills first
7. **Solving studies** — 3-5 days of intensive study solving for calculation breakthroughs

### The Relationship Core

1. **Deep diagnosis of each student's specific thinking errors**
2. **Radical honesty and trust between coach and student**
3. **Emotional investment** — Coach must care beyond the technical
4. **Parental alignment** — Parents must understand and support the method
5. **Group learning** — Mixed-level groups accelerate learning for all

### What Makes Ramesh Different

A ChessBase review captured the essence: *"His approach is more of a dialogue about chess and the underlying thought patterns — less about concrete variations and more about a fundamental understanding of the game, how to think, which positional aspects are relevant, and how to arrive at candidate moves and plans."*

The Chess Chatter Substack analyzed the Indian school (with Ramesh as its primary representative): *"The Indian school isn't a rejection of the Soviet tradition. It's an evolution of it, with the emphasis shifted from analysis-as-output to thinking-process-as-skill."*

And perhaps the most important characterization: *"Ramesh's method is Socratic."*
