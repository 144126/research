## Executive Summary

This report examines three distinct chess training approaches developed by Grandmasters Boris Gulko (USA, f.k.a. USSR), Igor Smirnov (Ukraine), and Davorin Kuljasevic (Croatia). Each represents a fundamentally different philosophy of how chess skill is acquired.

**Boris Gulko** — a Soviet-American Grandmaster who uniquely won both the USSR and US Championships and holds a plus score against Garry Kasparov — brings formal academic psychology (Master's degree from Moscow State University) to chess training. His approach, documented in *Lessons with a Grandmaster* (2011) and *Analyzing the Chess Mind* (2022), focuses on "piece harmony" over calculation, the psychological dimensions of decision-making, and Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) techniques for managing tournament pressure. His core argument: tactics are symptoms of strategic health, not isolated events.

**Igor Smirnov** — a Ukrainian Grandmaster who earned the IM title at 13 and GM at 20 — founded the Remote Chess Academy (RCA), a commercial online platform with 940K+ YouTube subscribers and 92K+ enrolled students. His system emphasizes practical thinking algorithms ("the 3 simple rules," "the Principle of the Least Active Piece") and a structured curriculum (the "Super Pack") that replaces scattered learning with a step-by-step training path. Independent endorsements from GM Jaan Ehlvest and student testimonials reporting 300-500 point rating gains suggest genuine efficacy, though the marketing-driven presentation attracts skepticism.

**Davorin Kuljasevic** — a Croatian GM and FIDE Trainer (2539 FIDE) with a Master's in Finance from Texas Tech — authored the definitive modern guide to self-directed chess study. His *How to Study Chess on Your Own* (2021) is less a training method and more a meta-methodology: it teaches players *how to learn* rather than what to learn. Its 15 systematically categorized study methods, structured study-plan creation framework, and three-volume workbook series represent the most academically rigorous approach of the three. His earlier *Beyond Material* (2019) — a FIDE Boleslavsky-Averbakh Award finalist — explores the relationship between material, time, and space as the three fundamental chess currencies.

**Key synthesis finding:** All three systems converge on the insufficiency of calculation-only training, but diverge sharply on the solution. Gulko prescribes psychological self-awareness and piece-harmony intuition; Smirnov prescribes algorithmic thinking rules and structured curricula; Kuljasevic prescribes systematic self-study methodology. These are not contradictory but complementary — they address different layers of the skill-acquisition problem (psychological, tactical-strategic, and meta-cognitive respectively).

## Boris Gulko: Psychological Chess Training

### 1.1 Background and Credentials

Boris Franzevich Gulko (born 1947) is a Soviet-American Grandmaster with an extraordinary dual claim to fame: he is the only player ever to win both the Soviet Chess Championship (1977, shared with Iosif Dorfman) and the U.S. Chess Championship (1994, 1999). He is also one of the very few players with a plus score against Garry Kasparov (3 wins, 4 draws, 1 loss from 1978-2001). His peak rating was 2644 (January 2000), placing him at world #14.

What distinguishes Gulko from virtually all other Grandmaster trainers is his formal academic training in psychology. He earned a Master's degree in Psychology from Moscow State University and worked for three years there as a research scientist, publishing several scientific articles on psychology in Russian. This formal background is exceptionally rare among elite chess players.

Gulko's life also included seven years as a Soviet "refusenik" — after winning the USSR Championship in 1977, he applied to emigrate to Israel and was denied permission. He and his wife (WGM Anna Akhsharumova) were barred from top-level competition until glasnost, a period he describes as devastating to his chess career but one he does not regret. This experience of political persecution and resilience informs his approach to the psychological "will to win" in chess.

### 1.2 The Psychological Approach to Chess Training

Gulko's chess psychology rests on a foundational claim, borrowed from Alexander Alekhine: **"Psychology is the most important factor in chess."** In his 2022 book *Analyzing the Chess Mind* (co-authored with Dr. Joel R. Sneed, Professor of Psychology at CUNY), Gulko applies formal psychological frameworks — particularly Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) — to chess training.

The core insight is that chess is not purely an objective game of calculation but a **human struggle** where cognitive distortions, emotional states, and personality traits directly affect move quality. Gulko and Sneed identify specific cognitive distortions common among chess players:

- **Catastrophizing:** After a mistake, the player spirals into "I'm an idiot" thinking, leading to premature resignation or desperate play
- **Perfectionism:** The belief that every move must be perfect, causing analysis paralysis and time pressure
- **Playing the rating:** Deferring to higher-rated opponents (playing too passively) or underestimating lower-rated ones (playing too carelessly)
- **Fear of winning:** The paralyzing pressure that arises when a winning position is finally achieved — the fear of "messing it up" becomes greater than the fear of losing

These are not presented as philosophical observations but as clinically identifiable patterns that can be addressed through specific CBT techniques. The three-part CBT model (Thoughts → Feelings → Behaviors) is applied directly to chess: negative thoughts about your position create negative emotions, which lead to poor behavioral choices at the board.

### 1.3 Targeting the "Subconscious" in Training

Gulko's approach to the subconscious operates at two levels:

**Level 1: Pattern Recognition Without Forcing.** Gulko argues that Grandmasters do not calculate more than amateurs in most positions — they *see* more. They have internalized thousands of positional patterns to the point where the correct plan feels obvious. This is what he calls the **"harmony" sense** — an unconscious feel for when pieces are working together correctly. The training method is not to consciously memorize patterns but to *immerse* in well-played games until the patterns become second nature. This aligns with the Chase & Simon (1973) chunking theory of chess expertise.

**Level 2: Awareness of Subconscious Emotional States.** Gulko teaches players to monitor their emotional state during games and recognize when subconscious biases are affecting decision-making. The training drill: during a game, when you notice a sudden feeling of anxiety or dread, ask yourself "What was just going through my mind?" The answer (e.g., "If I lose this game, my rating drops below 1800") reveals the cognitive distortion that needs to be challenged.

### 1.4 "Feeling the Position" — What It Means and How It's Trained

"Feeling the position" in Gulko's system means evaluating a position holistically rather than analytically — sensing the harmony or disharmony of piece placement before calculating specific variations. In the dialogue with co-author Sneed in *Lessons with a Grandmaster*, this is illustrated in a key exchange about Gulko's game against Bareev (1991):

**Sneed:** "I'm looking for a tactical solution here. Is there a sequence that wins a pawn or gains an advantage?"

**Gulko:** "You are looking for a result before you have created the conditions for it... In chess, the most important thing is the harmony of your pieces. If your pieces are in harmony, the tactics will find themselves. You must not look for a move; you must look for the right way to place your pieces."

The training method for developing this "feel":
1. **Analyze without engines first.** Spend 15-20 minutes per critical position describing what *feels right* and *feels wrong* about piece placement before calculating.
2. **Ask the prophylactic question.** Before planning your own moves, fully articulate what your opponent wants. This is trained through deliberate practice: in every position, force yourself to verbalize the opponent's best plan before considering your own.
3. **Practice piece-rerouting.** Exercises where the goal is not to find the best tactical shot but to find the best square for each piece over the next 3-5 moves.

### 1.5 Specific Drills and Methods

Based on the available materials, Gulko's specific training methods include:

1. **The Socratic Dialogue.** Both *Lessons with a Grandmaster* books are structured as dialogues between Gulko (the master) and Sneed (the amateur). This format is itself a training method: the student reads the position, makes their own decision, then compares their thought process to the dialogue. The reader is forced to see the gap between amateur calculation patterns and Grandmaster positional understanding.

2. **Game Simulation.** Rather than passively reading annotations, Gulko has the student play through master games move by move, deciding on each move before seeing the game continuation. This trains the decision-making process under realistic conditions.

3. **The "So What?" Drill.** For each opening move, the student must articulate the specific middlegame consequence. If you play d4 and c4, you must explain the resulting pawn structure tension, which piece squares become available, and what the endgame will look like. This prevents "theoretic blindness" — memorizing openings without understanding them.

4. **Prophylaxis Training.** Practice identifying the opponent's "ideal" move 2-3 moves before it becomes possible, then finding a move that prevents it. Common prophylactic moves (Kh1, a3, h3) are studied not as generic habits but as specific answers to concrete threats.

5. **Endgame-Seeding Awareness.** A specific drill where the player looks at a middlegame position and asks: "Which endgame does this position want to become?" This trains the "continuous narrative" view of the game rather than treating phases as isolated.

6. **CBT Self-Monitoring.** During and after games, players are trained to identify and log cognitive distortions using a structured worksheet:
   - What was the situation?
   - What automatic thought came to mind?
   - How did it make you feel?
   - What is the objective evidence?
   - What is a more balanced thought?

### 1.6 How Gulko's Approach Differs from Calculation-Focused Training

The fundamental difference is philosophical:

| Traditional Calculation Training | Gulko's Psychological Approach |
|--------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| "Find the best move" | "Understand the position" |
| Engine-centric analysis | Self-awareness + pattern immersion |
| Tactical puzzles in isolation | Positional harmony as the context for tactics |
| Memorize opening variations | Understand the "So What?" of each opening |
| Study endgame theory | Seed endgame awareness from the middlegame |
| Analyze games with engine | Analyze games with the prophylactic question |
| Focus on opponent's mistakes | Focus on your cognitive distortions |

Gulko explicitly warns against what he calls the **"analytical trap"** — excessive reliance on engine-led calculation that atomizes chess understanding into fragmented tactical puzzles. In his view, this produces players who can calculate five-move forcing sequences but remain blind to the underlying piece coordination that makes those sequences possible. His direct quotes from top US Championship players in *Analyzing the Chess Mind* reflect this consensus: watch games without engines (Wesley So, Nazi Paikidze), don't use computers (Carissa Yip), calculate lines yourself.

## Igor Smirnov: Remote Chess Academy & The "Essence" System

### 2.1 Background and Credentials

Igor Smirnov (born 1987, Sevastopol, Ukraine) is a Ukrainian Grandmaster (2008) who reached International Master at age 13 — one of the youngest in the world at that time. He won the Ukrainian Junior Championship (2002), took 1st prize at the Kasparov Cup (2009, presented by Garry Kasparov himself), and reached a peak rating of 2505 (January 2009).

After 2009, Smirnov shifted from competitive play to teaching, founding the **Remote Chess Academy (RCA)** . As of June 2026, the RCA YouTube channel has surpassed 940,000 subscribers and claims 92,000+ students enrolled across its courses, from 195+ countries. Smirnov also holds a Master's degree in psychology, which he cites as foundational to his chess education system.

### 2.2 The "Three Core Strategic Currencies" (Space, Time, Material)

While Smirnov's system does not use this exact phrase in its branding (the phrase appears more prominently in Kuljasevic's *Beyond Material*), Smirnov's teaching does revolve around a practical hierarchy of chess values. In his "7 Keys to Victory" series and free masterclasses, he teaches:

1. **Attack** (the priority) — The most important thing in chess is to attack your opponent. This means focusing your attention on the opponent's half of the board and asking: "How can I go to that territory and make some damage?"

2. **Activity/Activation** (the preparation for attack) — When no direct attack is possible, find the piece which is the least active and improve its position. This is Smirnov's signature principle: **"The Principle of the Least Active Piece."**

3. **Limitation** (the flip side of activity) — Restrict the opponent's pieces to the smallest possible number of squares. "Who is going to be the attacker and who the defender depends on the activity of your forces."

These three principles map loosely to the classical triadic framework: material (attack to gain it), space (activity and center control), and time (initiative and tempo). However, Smirnov's contribution is reframing these abstract concepts as a **practical decision-making algorithm** rather than a theoretical framework for analysis.

### 2.3 The "Essence System" — Claims of Compressed Learning

Smirnov's core claim is that most chess training is **ineffective** because it does not address how the human brain actually processes chess positions. In his own words from his Lybrary.com biography:

> "I trained hard for about 6-8 hours a day, had many coaches and read a lot of chess books. But then it came to me that a lot of new information wasn't making my game stronger! The coaches offered me loads of different training systems, but they didn't know the answer to this simple question: 'How to make real progress and start to play chess better?'"

The "essence" of his system is the claim that chess improvement is not about accumulating more information (more opening lines, more tactical patterns) but about **restructuring the thinking process itself**. He identified the core problem as: players learn rules and principles but don't know *when* to apply them in actual games. The gap between knowing and doing is bridged by a **simple, hierarchical decision tree** that the player follows in every position:

1. Is there a direct attack? → Execute it
2. If no attack → Activate the least active piece
3. While doing so → Limit opponent's activity
4. Control the center as the foundation for activity

This is deliberately reductive. Smirnov's argument is that traditional chess training overloads the player with dozens of principles (development, castling, central control, piece activity, pawn structure, etc.) without a clear priority ranking. His system provides a **strict priority order** so the player never faces an empty choice.

The claim of "compressing years into months" rests on this simplification: if you eliminate the time wasted on ineffective study methods (passive reading, random puzzle-solving without clear goals, opening memorization without understanding themes), and replace them with a structured thinking algorithm, the improvement rate accelerates dramatically.

### 2.4 Specific Methods for Rapid Improvement

Smirnov's "Super Pack" curriculum is the most comprehensive expression of his system. It includes courses on:

1. **GM Secrets** — Foundational thinking principles and strategic rules
2. **Champion Psychology** — Psychological preparation, managing pressure, building confidence
3. **The Grandmaster's Positional Understanding** — Deep positional concepts and strategic evaluation
4. **Your Winning Plan** — Middlegame planning and converting advantages
5. **Grandmaster's Opening Laboratory (1 & 2)** — Opening repertoire building with general principles
6. **Calculate Till Mate** — Calculation training and tactical vision
7. **Self-Taught Grandmaster** — Methods for independent study and training
8. **An Endgame Expert** — Endgame technique and theoretical knowledge
9. **How to Beat Titled Players** — Strategies for defeating stronger opponents

Specific methods emphasized across these courses:

- **The 3 Simple Rules (to Reach 2000):** A free masterclass that distills the entire system into three actionable rules. The specific rules are: (1) Always look for the attack first, (2) Activate your least active piece, (3) Restrict your opponent's best piece.

- **The "Terminator" Concept:** Identifying and eliminating the opponent's most important defensive piece, then using the resulting weakness to launch a decisive attack.

- **30-Minute Study Plan (Busy Adult):** A time-efficient daily routine designed for players who cannot dedicate hours to training. The plan prescribes specific activities in a fixed order: tactical puzzles (10 min), opening review (10 min), game analysis (10 min).

- **Training from Practical Games:** Rather than studying theoretical positions, Smirnov emphasizes learning from real game examples where the thinking process is demonstrated move by move.

### 2.5 Reported Student Results

The RCA website and masterclass page document specific testimonials. These are vendor-sourced (company marketing), so should be evaluated with appropriate skepticism:

- **Kurt (USA):** Chess.com rating jumped from 1225 to 1716 (491-point increase) in one year. "I began studying chess seriously for the first time at age 52."

- **Chandra:** "After studying GM Secrets and Champion Psychology... my tournament results improved dramatically — I even finished as runner-up."

- **Vinay (parent of student):** Son jumped from 1100 to 1400 FIDE and "won 1st place (Class E) at the 2025 SuperNationals in Florida."

- **GM Karthik V AP (India):** "After a few months of training, I started beating IMs and GMs in official tournaments!" Later became a Grandmaster, peak 2594 FIDE. (Note: causal attribution to Smirnov's training vs. other factors is unclear.)

Independent user discussions (Chess.com forums, Reddit) provide more mixed perspectives. Positive reviews emphasize the practical utility of the thinking system. Criticisms focus on:
- Aggressive marketing tactics ("salesy" landing pages, limited-time offers)
- The "organization-like" feel — less personal than other coaches
- Over-reliance on hope-chess in short-form video content
- Some claims of "compressed learning" that are difficult to verify independently

Notably, GM **Jaan Ehlvest** (former elite player, once ranked behind only Kasparov, Karpov, and Salov in the World Cup series) published an endorsement of Smirnov's thinking system, praising it as "more practical" than Kotov or Silman and noting the "logical hierarchy" that makes it accessible to both beginners and advanced players.

### 2.6 Can the Method Work with Just a Board (No Screens)?

Smirnov's system is primarily delivered through video courses and digital materials hosted on the RCA platform (chess-teacher.com). However, the *principles* themselves — the priority-based decision tree, the concept of the least active piece, the termination of key enemy defenders — require no screens to apply. They are thinking frameworks that can be practiced with a physical board and pieces, or even during visualization exercises.

The study plan includes digital components (opening databases in PGN format, video analysis), but a motivated student could extract the thinking principles and apply them with traditional board study. The "Busy Adult Study Plan" is designed for minimal resource dependency.

## Davorin Kuljasevic: How to Study Chess on Your Own

### 3.1 Background and Credentials

Davorin Kuljasevic (born ~1987) is a Croatian Grandmaster (FIDE 2539) and FIDE Trainer who brings an unusual interdisciplinary background to chess: he holds both an undergraduate degree in Finance and Operations Management from the University of Texas at Dallas (2009) and a Master's degree in Finance from Texas Tech University (2011). This academic training in systematic analysis and structured frameworks is directly reflected in his chess books.

He played in the US Chess League (USCL) for the Dallas Destiny team (2007-2008), which became US champion in both years. His tournament wins include Castelfranco Veneto (2014), Split Open (2013), and Mitropa Cup (2013) with the Croatian national team.

Kuljasevic is the author of two highly regarded chess books published by New in Chess:
- *Beyond Material: Ignore the Face Value of Your Pieces and Discover the Importance of Time, Space and Psychology in Chess* (2019) — a FIDE Boleslavsky-Averbakh Award finalist
- *How to Study Chess on Your Own: Creating a Plan That Works… and Sticking to It!* (2021) — 368 pages, with a three-volume workbook series following (2023-2025)

He has produced instructional content in collaboration with New in Chess, Chessable, Chess.com, ChessBase, ICC, Modern Chess, thechessworld.com, Forward Chess, Chessmood, and the Kasparov Chess Foundation. He is also a personal coach, accepting students rated 1600+ FIDE at $100/hour.

### 3.2 The "Beyond Fundamentals" Approach

Kuljasevic's "beyond fundamentals" approach is most fully articulated in *Beyond Material*. The book's premise is that most chess players are trapped in a **material-centric mindset** — evaluating positions primarily by the material balance — and need to develop a framework where material, time, and space are treated as **equivalent and interconnected currencies** rather than material being the only "real" value.

From the book's preface:
> "The materialistic value system generally works well in chess. However, it is also sort of like 'The Matrix' — obscuring deeper chess truths by its outwardly pleasing design."

The six chapters systematically dismantle material-centric thinking:

1. **Attachment to Material** — Why players cling to material even when sacrificing is better; psychological barriers to dynamic play
2. **Relative Value of Material** — How piece values fluctuate depending on position (a knight on an outpost is worth more than 3 pawns; a trapped bishop is worth less)
3. **Time Beats Material** — When initiative, tempo, and development are worth sacrificing material for
4. **Space Beats Material** — When mobility, control of key squares, and positional domination are worth material sacrifices
5. **Psychology of Non-Materialism** — The emotional and cognitive aspects of sacrificing; risk tolerance; putting psychological pressure on opponents
6. **Is It Good to Be Greedy?** — A dialectical examination: when holding material is correct vs. when it's a mistake

The book features 107 annotated games, many of them previously unpublished, with a substantial number from the author's own practice.

### 3.3 How Masters "See" Plans While Amateurs Calculate

Kuljasevic's perspective on the perceptual gap between masters and amateurs aligns with the established research tradition (de Groot 1965, Chase & Simon 1973) but translates it into practical training advice. His key insight:

> Masters don't calculate more than amateurs — they calculate **more selectively**. The master's intuition is not mystical; it is the result of having seen thousands of positions where certain plans work and others don't, to the point where the evaluation feels immediate.

In his "Grandmaster's Thinking" course (8.5 hours, 15 lessons, produced with TheChessWorld.com), he identifies specific thinking traits that differentiate Grandmasters:

1. **Flexible thinking** — The ability to switch between concrete calculation and abstract planning depending on position type
2. **Non-materialism** — The willingness to sacrifice material based on positional compensation rather than guaranteed recovery
3. **Prophylactic thinking** — Constant awareness of opponent's resources, not just one's own plans
4. **Strategic (schematic) thinking** — The ability to form long-term plans based on abstract positional features
5. **Critical moment identification** — Recognizing when the position requires deep calculation vs. when a simple plan suffices

The most critical of these is the distinction between **concrete thinking** (suitable for open, tactical positions) and **abstract thinking** (suitable for closed, strategic positions). Amateurs tend to apply one mode universally; masters switch fluidly depending on the position.

### 3.4 Specific Training Methods Prescribed

*How to Study Chess on Your Own* (2021) is structured as a comprehensive self-study manual. Its most notable feature is Chapter 2, which categorizes **15 distinct study methods** and rates each on three dimensions:

| Study Method | Practical Relevance | Study Intensity | Long-Term Learning |
|-------------|-------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Light analysis | Medium | Low | Low |
| Deep analysis | High | High | High |
| Playing through games | Medium | Medium | Medium |
| Game simulation | High | High | High |
| Sparring | High | High | High |
| Reading books | Low-Medium | Low | Medium |
| Watching videos | Low | Low | Low |
| Solving tactics | High | Medium | High |
| Studying endgames | High | Medium | High |
| Opening study | High | Medium-High | Medium |
| Reviewing your games | Very High | High | Very High |
| Using engines | Medium | Low | Low |
| Playing serious games | Very High | N/A | High |
| Blitz practice | Low-Medium | Low | Low |
| Visualization training | High | High | High |

This framework is notable for its **honesty**: watching videos and reading books are rated as having low study intensity and low-to-medium long-term learning potential, despite being among the most common study methods. "Reviewing your own games" is rated highest across all three dimensions.

Other key training methods prescribed:

1. **Game Simulation.** The single most important method: take the position from a master game, spend 15-20 minutes analyzing it as if it were your own tournament game (no engine), decide on a plan, then compare your analysis to the actual game continuation. This trains decision-making under realistic conditions.

2. **"Dynamize" Tactical Training.** Rather than solving random puzzles, Kuljasevic advocates for thematic tactical study: focus on one tactical motif (e.g., deflection, discovered attack) for an entire session, and practice identifying the *conditions* that make the motif possible, not just the solution.

3. **Structured Endgame Study.** Not just memorizing theoretical positions, but understanding endgame principles through the "3P" framework: Pawn structure, Piece activity, and Passive/active King.

4. **Deep Game Analysis.** Not engine-assisted blitz-throughs, but patient, move-by-move analysis where you articulate the purpose of each move before checking with the engine.

5. **Opening Repertoire as a Mirror.** Use your opening study to identify strategic preferences and weaknesses, not just to build a "ready-made" repertoire.

### 3.5 Bridging the Perceptual Gap

Kuljasevic's approach to bridging the perceptual gap between amateur and master thinking is systematic rather than mystical:

**Step 1: Self-Diagnosis.** Identify which of the 15 study methods you actually use and how effective they are for your level. Most players over-rely on low-intensity methods (watching videos, blitz) and under-utilize high-intensity methods (deep analysis, game simulation, reviewing own games).

**Step 2: Level-Appropriate Resource Selection.** The book provides detailed guidance matching study resources to playing level (beginner, intermediate, advanced, master). A common mistake: studying material meant for 2200+ players when rated 1400, or vice versa.

**Step 3: Creating a Study Plan.** Chapter 9 provides a structured template for creating a personalized study plan, including:
- Weekly schedule (hours per day, distribution across areas)
- Phase-based planning (opening preparation phase, tactics phase, endgame phase)
- Progress review and adjustment

**Step 4: Becoming an Independent Student.** The ultimate goal is not to follow instructions but to "actively inquire, research, collect, analyze, experiment, challenge assumptions, challenge yourself, and find your own ways in chess study."

### 3.6 Views on Compressed Learning

Kuljasevic is notably more cautious than Smirnov about claims of rapid or compressed learning. He does not promise to compress years into months. Instead, his central message is that most players waste a significant portion of their study time on ineffective methods, and that **reducing waste** accelerates progress — but does not eliminate the need for sustained effort.

From the book's preface:
> "I have seen many talented and ambitious players who had the right intentions, and yet managed to go about chess study in the wrong ways... They lacked a clear direction in their study, pursued too many study goals at the same time, did not have a structured approach to it... they are not even aware that this might be the main reason behind their lack of improvement."

His view could be summarized as: **optimization > compression**. You cannot meaningfully shortcut the 10,000+ hours required for mastery, but you can ensure that every hour counts by using evidence-based study methods and avoiding common traps.

## Cross-Comparison & Synthesis

### 4.1 Where They Agree

All three systems converge on several critical points:

1. **Calculation is necessary but insufficient.** Pure tactical calculation — the ability to see forcing sequences 6-7 moves deep — does not make a strong player. Strategic understanding, positional awareness, and psychological resilience are equally or more important.

2. **Passive study methods are overrated.** Watching videos, reading annotated games without engaging, and using engines as crutches all receive criticism across all three systems. Active engagement — making decisions and comparing them to master decisions — is the universal prescription.

3. **Self-awareness is essential.** Gulko frames this as cognitive behavioral self-monitoring, Smirnov as understanding your own thinking process, Kuljasevic as honest self-diagnosis of strengths and weaknesses. All three agree that the student must understand *how* they currently think before attempting to improve.

4. **Master thinking is not about brute force.** All three describe Grandmaster-level thinking as selective, efficient, and pattern-based rather than exhaustive calculation.

### 4.2 Where They Diverge

| Dimension | Gulko | Smirnov | Kuljasevic |
|-----------|-------|---------|------------|
| **Primary focus** | Psychology & piece harmony | Thinking algorithm & structured curriculum | Meta-learning & study methodology |
| **Training medium** | Books (dialogues) | Video courses (RCA platform) | Books + workbooks + open courses |
| **Role of psychology** | Central (CBT-trained) | Important (background in psych) | One component among many |
| **Claims about speed** | Modest | Ambitious ("compressed years") | Cautious ("optimize, don't shortcut") |
| **Target audience** | Intermediate to advanced | All levels (beginner to advanced) | Adult improvers, club to master |
| **Uniqueness** | Only GM with formal psych degree | Scalable commercial platform | Most systematic meta-approach |
| **Criticism profile** | Very little negative | "Too salesy," hard to verify claims | Highly respected, niche reach |

### 4.3 Potential Synergies

A training program that combined all three approaches would look like:

- **Foundation:** Kuljasevic's study methodology (how to learn, what to prioritize, how to avoid waste)
- **Framework:** Smirnov's thinking algorithm (the priority-based decision tree for in-game decisions)
- **Depth:** Gulko's psychological self-awareness (CBT for tournament play, harmony-based positional understanding)

This would address three different bottlenecks simultaneously: inefficient study habits (Kuljasevic), unclear in-game decision-making priorities (Smirnov), and psychological fragility under pressure (Gulko).

### 4.4 Weakest Evidence / Gaps

The following claims in this report are the least well-supported:

1. **Smirnov's compressed learning claims.** The claim that his system compresses "years into months" rests entirely on vendor-sourced testimonials. No independent controlled studies or third-party verification exists. Stronger evidence would require longitudinal tracking with control groups.

2. **Causal attribution of GM Karthik's success to Smirnov.** Karthik V AP became a Grandmaster and reached 2594 FIDE, and his testimonial attributes early-stage improvement to Smirnov's courses. However, correlation does not equal causation — elite players typically work with multiple coaches and training systems.

3. **Gulko's CBT effectiveness claims.** While Gulko and Sneed convincingly apply CBT principles to chess, there is no published empirical research measuring the effect of CBT-based chess training on rating improvement. The evidence is anecdotal (Sneed's personal improvement) and theoretical.
