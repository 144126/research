# DEEP RESEARCH COMMISSION

## Research Question

What is the minimum number of miles (or steps) a human must walk per day to stay healthy — grounded in the best available evidence from public health guidelines, large-scale epidemiological studies, and systematic reviews?

## Purpose & Context

This research matters because the "10,000 steps a day" rule is widely known but scientifically baseless — it originated as a 1960s Japanese marketing campaign for a pedometer called Manpo-kei. Meanwhile, official guidelines (CDC, WHO, AHA, NHS) recommend 150 minutes/week of moderate activity without specifying daily step or mileage targets. The public is caught between vague "move more" advice and an arbitrary 10,000-step goal. A clear, evidence-based answer to "how many miles must I actually walk?" has practical implications for billions of people.

The audience is a 54-year-old man with deep academic exposure who wants his 9-year-old child to also understand the report. Every hard concept must get an inline [imagine: ...] explanation.

### Decision Context
- What personal daily walking target should an average healthy adult aim for?
- What is the absolute minimum below which health risks significantly increase?
- How do these numbers change for older adults (65+), beginners, or people with chronic conditions?
- Where did 10,000 steps come from and has it misled public health messaging?

## Research Scope

### In Scope
1. Minimum effective daily walking dose for all-cause mortality reduction
2. Dose-response relationship between steps/miles and health outcomes
3. Origin and debunking of the 10,000-steps myth
4. Official guidelines: CDC, WHO, AHA, NHS, Australian, Canadian guidelines
5. Differences by age (<60 vs 60+)
6. Role of walking intensity (brisk vs. leisurely pace) vs. total volume
7. Economic burden of insufficient walking/physical inactivity
8. Global inactivity statistics

### Out of Scope
- Running, jogging, or vigorous exercise as alternatives to walking
- Specific disease management programs (cardiac rehab, diabetes prevention programs)
- Wearable device accuracy comparisons
- Walking for weight loss specifically (health vs. weight are distinct)
- Diet or sleep interactions with walking
- Step count targets for children/adolescents (focus on adults 18+)

### Timeframe
2020–2026 with emphasis on 2023–2026 large-scale meta-analyses and systematic reviews. Foundational papers pre-2020 included where historically important (e.g., origin of 10,000 steps).

### Geographic Focus
Global perspective with emphasis on US, UK, Australia, Canada, EU, and WHO guidelines.

## Required Dimensions of Investigation

1. **Technical/Mechanistic Exploration**
   - By what physiological mechanisms does walking reduce all-cause mortality? [imagine: what happens inside your body when you walk that keeps you alive longer?]

   - What is the dose-response curve shape (linear, logarithmic, threshold, plateau)?

2. **Historical Context & Evolution**
   - Where did 10,000 steps originate? Detailed story of the 1964 Manpo-kei pedometer marketing campaign
   - How did official guidelines evolve from 1990s to present?
   - Why has the "10,000 steps" meme persisted despite lack of evidence?

3. **Current State-of-Art (2023-2026)**
   - What do the largest and most recent meta-analyses say? (Lancet Public Health 2025 review, Banach et al. 2023, Harvard 2023 study)
   - What is the current consensus from major health authorities?
   - What are the age-stratified findings (younger vs. older adults)?
   - Latest 2025-2026 studies on step counts and health outcomes

4. **Quantitative Evidence**
   - What is the minimum step count showing statistically significant mortality reduction? (2,500? 4,000?)
   - At what step count do benefits plateau for different age groups?
   - What is the relative risk reduction per 500/1,000-step increment?
   - Global physical inactivity prevalence and its economic cost
   - Percentage of population meeting various walking thresholds

5. **Stakeholder Analysis**
   - Major health authorities: CDC, WHO, AHA, NHS — what do they recommend and how do they differ?
   - Influence of fitness tracker companies (Fitbit, Apple, Garmin) on 10,000-step norm

   - Insurance and employer wellness programs using step targets

6. **Competing Approaches Comparison**
   - Time-based (150 min/week) vs. step-based (X,000 steps) vs. distance-based (X miles) — which is most evidence-backed and practical?

   - Does walking intensity (cadence, pace) matter independently of total steps?

7. **Criticisms & Limitations**
   - Key limitations of step-counting research (observational nature, confounding, measurement error)
   - Are the studies correlational rather than causal?
   - What confounding factors are inadequately controlled for?
   - Criticism of the 150 min/week guideline as insufficiently specific

8. **Contrarian & Heterodox Perspectives**
   - Is the entire "minimum daily requirement" framing flawed? (i.e., should we think in terms of weekly volume, variability, or replacing sedentary time?)
   - Arguments that the evidence doesn't support any firm minimum
   - Do the health benefits of walking apply equally across all populations?
   - Could focusing on a single number be counterproductive?

9. **Future Trajectories & Predictions**

11. **Geographic & Geopolitical Variation**
