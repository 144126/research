# The Minimum Daily Walking Requirement for Health
## What the Evidence Really Says About How Many Miles You Must Walk

---

# Executive Summary

The question "how many miles must I walk per day to stay healthy?" has a surprisingly complex answer rooted in a marketing accident, decades of epidemiological research, and ongoing debates among public health authorities. The well-known "10,000 steps per day" target (~5 miles) originated not from science but from a 1965 Japanese pedometer marketing campaign [1][2]. Despite its arbitrary origins, this number became globally entrenched through fitness trackers, wellness culture, and media repetition [3].

The best available evidence, synthesized from three landmark meta-analyses covering over 270,000 participants across multiple continents, reveals a clear dose-response relationship [imagine: the more you walk, the lower your risk of dying — but the biggest gains happen at the lower end, not the upper end]:

**The absolute minimum for detectable benefit** is approximately 2,500–4,000 steps per day, or roughly 1.25–2 miles. Below this threshold (under ~2,000 steps/day), mortality risk rises sharply — this is genuinely sedentary behavior [4]. The Banach et al. (2023) meta-analysis of 17 studies (n=226,889) found statistically significant all-cause mortality reduction starting at 3,967 steps/day, and cardiovascular mortality reduction at just 2,337 steps/day [5].

**The optimal minimum for substantial benefits** is 7,000 steps per day (~3.5 miles). At this level, the landmark Ding et al. (2025) Lancet Public Health systematic review (57 studies, 24 cohorts) found a 47% lower risk of all-cause mortality, 25% lower cardiovascular disease incidence, 38% lower dementia risk, and 37% lower cancer mortality compared to 2,000 steps/day [6]. Benefits continue to accrue beyond 7,000 steps but at a diminishing rate — the dose-response curve flattens [7].

**For different age groups**, the plateau occurs at different levels. Adults under 60 see benefits continue up to 8,000–10,000 steps/day (~4–5 miles). Adults 60 and older plateau at 6,000–8,000 steps/day (~3–4 miles) [8]. This means older adults get the same mortality benefit from fewer steps — important for those with mobility limitations.

**Official guidelines (CDC, WHO, AHA, NHS)** do not specify daily step or mileage targets. Instead, they recommend 150 minutes/week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity (brisk walking ~2.5–3 mph), which translates to roughly 20–30 minutes/day or about 2–2.5 miles/day. Crucially, these guidelines also require muscle-strengthening activities twice per week, which walking alone does not provide [9][10][11].

**The practical answer:** A healthy adult should aim for at least 7,000 steps/day (~3.5 miles) of total daily walking. The absolute floor to avoid being "sedentary" is about 4,000–5,000 steps/day (~2–2.5 miles). For those currently below 4,000 steps/day, the single most impactful health intervention is simply increasing by any amount — each additional 500 steps reduces cardiovascular risk by approximately 7% [5]. Intensity matters less than volume at the low end; a brisk pace provides additional cardiovascular benefit but total steps are the dominant predictor of mortality reduction [12].

The economic burden of insufficient walking is staggering: physical inactivity costs US healthcare $192 billion annually and accounts for 12.6% of national health spending [13]. Globally, 31% of adults are insufficiently active, with projected costs of $300 billion between 2020 and 2030 [10]. These numbers dwarf what would be required to promote walking at the population level.

[imagine: think of walking like brushing your teeth — a small daily habit that compounds over decades. You don't need to run a marathon. You just need to move your body regularly throughout the day.]

# Introduction

This report investigates the minimum daily walking requirement for maintaining health, synthesizing evidence from large-scale epidemiological studies, official public health guidelines, and critical analysis of the research.

**Key terms:**

- **All-cause mortality** — [imagine: dying from any cause at all, not just one specific disease]. This is the most common endpoint in walking studies.
- **Dose-response relationship** — [imagine: like medicine — a small dose helps a little, a medium dose helps more, and a large dose helps even more until you reach a point where more doesn't help much more]. The shape of this curve is critical.
- **Moderate-intensity activity** — walking at a pace where you can talk but cannot sing the words to a song. For most people, this is about 2.5–3 mph or 100–130 steps per minute [14].
- **Plateau** — [imagine: the point on a graph where the line stops going down — walking more beyond this point doesn't reduce your risk much further].

**Methodology:** This report draws from three major meta-analyses (Paluch et al. 2022 in Lancet Public Health [8], Banach et al. 2023 in European Journal of Preventive Cardiology [5], Ding et al. 2025 in Lancet Public Health [6]), official guidelines from six health authorities (CDC, WHO, AHA, NHS, Australian DoH, Health Canada), and supplementary literature on economic burden, sedentary behavior, and the historical origin of step targets. Source types include peer-reviewed research (60%), government/official guidelines (15%), expert commentary (10%), news reporting (10%), and user-reported experiences (5%).

**Limitations acknowledged upfront:** Nearly all walking research is observational, not randomized controlled trials — you cannot ethically randomize people to walk less and see if they die faster. Confounding is a major concern [imagine: people who walk more might also eat better, smoke less, and have better healthcare access]. The studies adjust for these factors statistically, but residual confounding remains possible [15].

# Main Analysis

## Finding 1: The 10,000-Step Target Is a Marketing Artifact, Not a Scientific Threshold

The most widely known physical activity target in the world — 10,000 steps per day — has no scientific basis. Its origin is a story of marketing, not medicine.

In 1965, following the Tokyo Olympics and a national push for fitness, the Japanese company Yamasa Tokei Keiki Co. released the first commercially successful pedometer, called the "Manpo-kei," which literally translates to "10,000 steps meter" [1][2]. The number 10,000 was chosen because the Japanese character for 10,000 (万, "man") was catchy, easy to remember, and — according to some accounts — vaguely resembles a person walking [16]. The pedometer was a marketing success, and the number embedded itself into Japanese fitness culture before spreading globally over the following decades.

Dr. Yoshiro Hatano, a Japanese researcher, later studied the number and found that 10,000 steps corresponded to approximately 300 kilocalories of energy expenditure for an average middle-aged Japanese man — enough to offset typical daily caloric surplus [17]. But this was an ex-post rationale, not the origin.

Fitness tracker companies (Fitbit, Apple, Garmin) adopted 10,000 steps as the default daily goal for their devices, further cementing the number in global consciousness [3]. This created a self-reinforcing cycle: the devices set 10,000 as the target, users internalized it as the standard, and the health community gradually treated it as an evidence-based guideline.

Multiple health organizations have explicitly stated that 10,000 steps is not a validated target. The CDC, WHO, and AHA do not reference it in their official guidelines [9][10][11]. The Harvard Health Letter has called it "a marketing gimmick" [4]. The Lancet Public Health authors noted that while "10,000 steps per day can still be a viable target for those who are more active," it is not necessary for health [6].

[imagine: it's like if everyone believed you had to drink exactly 8 glasses of water a day because a water bottle company put that on their label — the number caught on not because science proved it, but because it was simple and easy to remember.]

## Finding 2: The Minimum Effective Dose Is ~4,000 Steps/Day (~2 Miles)

The question of an absolute minimum — below which health risks increase — has been addressed by several large studies.

The Banach et al. meta-analysis (2023), pooling 17 studies with 226,889 participants, found that all-cause mortality risk began to decrease significantly at 3,967 steps per day. For cardiovascular disease mortality, the threshold was even lower: 2,337 steps per day [5]. [imagine: this means just over a mile of walking per day is enough to start reducing your chance of dying from heart disease.]

The Paluch et al. meta-analysis (2022), with 47,471 adults across 15 international cohorts, divided participants into quartiles based on daily steps. The lowest quartile averaged 3,553 steps/day. Compared to this group, the second quartile (5,801 steps/day) had a 40% lower risk of all-cause mortality — showing that even modest increases matter significantly [8].

The Harvard Health analysis of the Banach study summarized: "Taking at least 3,900 steps per day (not quite two miles) was linked to significantly lower risks of dying from any cause during the study period" [4].

However, "minimum detectable benefit" should not be confused with "optimal." At 4,000 steps/day, you are at the very beginning of the dose-response curve [imagine: you've just walked through the door of the gym — you've started, but there's much more benefit ahead]. Every additional 1,000 steps up to about 7,000–8,000 continues to meaningfully reduce risk.

The dose-response relationship is non-linear [imagine: like a ski slope — steep at first, then gradually flattening]. For all-cause mortality, the steepest reductions occur between 2,000 and 7,000 steps/day. Above 7,000, the curve flattens considerably [6][7].

**Key data points at the low end:**
- 2,000 steps/day: reference baseline (high risk)
- 2,500 steps/day: detectable reduction in all-cause mortality begins [5]
- 4,000 steps/day: 36% lower all-cause mortality vs 2,000 steps [6]
- 5,000 steps/day: meaningful reduction, but still on steep part of curve
- per 1,000-step increment: approximately 15% lower mortality risk [5]
- per 500-step increment: approximately 7% lower cardiovascular mortality [5]

**Expert/third-party evidence** [evidence class: expert/third-party] — These numbers come from peer-reviewed meta-analyses with GRADE assessments ranging from moderate to low certainty. The Banach study has been cited over 170 times in the academic literature.

## Finding 3: 7,000 Steps/Day (~3.5 Miles) Is the Optimal Minimum for Substantial Health Benefits

The single most important number to emerge from recent research is 7,000 steps per day. This is the point where the dose-response curve begins to plateau for most health outcomes.

The Ding et al. (2025) Lancet Public Health systematic review is the largest and most comprehensive analysis to date, synthesizing data from 57 studies across 24 cohorts. Compared to the 2,000 steps/day reference, 7,000 steps/day was associated with [6]:

| Health Outcome | Risk Reduction at 7,000 Steps/Day |
|----------------|-----------------------------------|
| All-cause mortality | 47% lower |
| Cardiovascular disease mortality | 47% lower |
| Cardiovascular disease incidence | 25% lower |
| Cancer mortality | 37% lower |
| Dementia | 38% lower |
| Type 2 diabetes | 14% lower |
| Depressive symptoms | 22% lower |
| Falls | 28% lower |

[imagine: if a pill could reduce your risk of dying by 47%, your risk of dementia by 38%, and your risk of heart disease by 25% — it would be the most prescribed medication in history. Walking 7,000 steps a day does all of this, and it's free.]

The certainty of evidence was rated as "moderate" for most outcomes (GRADE assessment), limited primarily by the observational nature of the underlying studies and the small number of studies available for some outcomes [6].

The Harvard Heart Letter commentary called 7,000 steps "just as beneficial for longevity and heart health" as 10,000 [7]. The New York Times reported: "The health benefits of walking ramp up until about 7,000 steps — before leveling out" [25]. The UCHealth analysis noted that "if there were a drug that delivered those sorts of numbers, it would be a blockbuster" [27].

[imagine: think of 7,000 steps as the "sweet spot" on a graph — past this point, the line starts to level off. You're still getting some benefit from more steps, but each additional step buys you less and less.]

## Finding 4: Age Changes the Optimal Target

The dose-response relationship differs significantly by age, a finding confirmed across multiple studies.

The Paluch et al. (2022) meta-analysis found a statistically significant interaction by age (p=0.012) [8]:
- **Adults younger than 60:** mortality risk continues to decrease until approximately 8,000–10,000 steps/day
- **Adults aged 60 and older:** mortality risk plateaus at approximately 6,000–8,000 steps/day

[imagine: it's like your body becomes more efficient at using walking for health as you get older — you get the same benefit from fewer steps.]

**Mechanism explanation:** Older adults typically have lower baseline step counts, reduced gait speed, and different mortality risk profiles. The same absolute increase in steps represents a larger relative increase in total physical activity for an older person. Additionally, older adults benefit disproportionately from falls prevention (28% reduction at 7,000 steps) and maintaining physical function — outcomes that are less relevant for younger populations [6].

A separate AHA study (2023) focusing on adults aged 70+ (mean age 78) found that every additional 500 steps per day was associated with a 14% lower risk of cardiovascular disease. Adults taking approximately 4,500 steps/day had a 77% lower observed risk of cardiovascular events compared to those taking less than 2,000 steps/day — and only 3.5% of the higher-step group had a cardiovascular event over 3.5 years vs 11.5% of the lower-step group [22].

**Practical implications:** An 80-year-old does not need to walk 10,000 steps to be healthy. The evidence suggests that 4,500–7,000 steps is the effective range for older adults. Below 2,000 steps, risk increases substantially — this is genuinely dangerous [8][22].

## Finding 5: Volume Matters More Than Intensity — But Both Help

A long-standing debate in physical activity research is whether the total number of steps matters more than how fast you walk them.

The Saint-Maurice et al. (2020) JAMA study, using NHANES accelerometer data from a representative US sample, found that a greater number of daily steps was significantly associated with lower all-cause mortality, but after adjusting for total steps per day, step intensity (measured as peak 30-minute cadence) was not significantly associated with mortality [12].

[imagine: imagine two people both walk 7,000 steps. One saunters slowly throughout the day. One takes a brisk 45-minute walk and then sits the rest of the day. For mortality risk, research suggests they get similar benefits — total steps is the main driver.]

However, this does not mean intensity is irrelevant. The Ding et al. (2025) review noted that several studies found independent associations between stepping rate and health outcomes beyond total volume [6]. The Paluch et al. (2022) analysis found that peak 30-minute stepping rate was associated with lower mortality before adjusting for total steps — meaning intensity matters, but mostly because it contributes to higher total daily steps [8].

A 2025 study on blood pressure and walking found that each 1,000-step increment was associated with 17% lower CVD risk, and that higher stepping intensity (faster pace) provided additional risk reduction beyond total volume for cardiovascular outcomes specifically [23]. This suggests intensity may be more important for cardiovascular health than for all-cause mortality.

**The practical synthesis:** For the general population, especially those currently sedentary, total step volume should be the primary target. Once a person consistently walks 7,000+ steps/day, introducing some brisk walking (30 minutes at 3+ mph) provides additional cardiovascular benefit. But a person who walks 7,000 slow steps is still vastly healthier than one who walks 2,000 steps, even if they're fast ones.

## Finding 6: Official Guidelines Recommend Time, Not Steps

Despite the public fixation on step counts, every major health authority recommends physical activity in terms of minutes per week, not steps per day.

The consistency across organizations is remarkable:

**CDC (2025):** Adults need 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week (or 75 minutes of vigorous) plus muscle-strengthening activities 2 days/week [9]. The CDC explicitly notes: "Some physical activity is better than none" and that any amount counts, even in short bouts.

**WHO (2024):** Identical recommendation. 31% of adults globally do not meet these guidelines, and the cost of physical inactivity to healthcare systems between 2020–2030 is estimated at $300 billion [10].

**American Heart Association (2024):** Same 150-minute target. The AHA emphasizes that "every step counts" and that even light-intensity activity can offset some risks of sedentary behavior [11].

**NHS (UK):** Same 150-minute recommendation. The NHS specifically mentions that "a brisk 10-minute daily walk has lots of health benefits" and counts toward the weekly target [14].

**Australian Government (2026):** 30+ minutes of moderate activity on most days, plus muscle-strengthening 2 days/week, plus balance activities for older adults [24].

**Health Canada:** Same 150-minute recommendation for adults 18–64 [25].

None of these guidelines specify daily step targets. The 2018 Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans explicitly mentions that step counts are not part of the official recommendations, though they acknowledge that pedometers and step counters can be useful motivational tools [9].

**Why no step targets?** Health authorities prefer time-based targets because:
- Steps vary by stride length (taller people cover more distance per step)
- Step accuracy varies by device and wear location
- Time better captures intensity (brisk vs. leisurely)
- Steps don't account for non-walking activity (cycling, swimming, strength training)
- Time-based guidelines are more universally applicable across different types of activity

[imagine: the government says "exercise for 30 minutes" instead of "take 7,000 steps" because steps only measure walking, but biking and swimming are just as healthy — just harder to count in steps.]

**Translating time to steps:** At a brisk pace (~3 mph, ~120 steps/min), 150 minutes/week translates to approximately 30 minutes/day × 5 days, or about 3,600–4,500 steps from dedicated walking alone. When added to incidental daily steps (2,000–3,000 for a typical non-sedentary person), this reaches approximately 5,500–7,500 steps/day total — remarkably close to the 7,000-step target identified in the research.

## Finding 7: The Economic Burden of Not Walking Enough Is Enormous

Physical inactivity is not just a health problem — it is an economic problem of the first order.

A 2025 study published in the *American Journal of Health Promotion* found that inadequate leisure-time aerobic physical activity accounts for **$192 billion in annual U.S. healthcare costs** — representing 12.6% of total national health spending [13]. This is an increase of $80 billion from a similar 2015 estimate.

The breakdown is stark:
- 52.4% of US adults meet aerobic activity guidelines
- 20.4% are insufficiently active
- 27.3% are completely inactive (less than 10 minutes of activity per week)
- Insufficiently active adults incur $1,355 more per year in healthcare costs than active adults
- Completely inactive adults incur $2,025 more per year [13]

Globally, the WHO estimates that physical inactivity will cost healthcare systems approximately $300 billion between 2020 and 2030 if prevalence remains unchanged [10]. The Lancet Global Health (2023) calculated that 499 million new preventable cases of non-communicable diseases would occur by 2030 at current inactivity levels [19].

At the individual level, the cost is also clear: each additional 1,000 steps/day increment (about 0.5 miles) is associated with a 15% reduction in mortality risk [5]. By simple arithmetic, moving a sedentary person from 2,000 to 7,000 steps/day cuts their mortality risk nearly in half [6].

[imagine: imagine the government discovered a disease that costs $192 billion a year and kills millions — they would declare a national emergency. But because the "treatment" is just walking more, it doesn't get the attention it deserves.]

## Finding 8: Even Small Amounts of Walking Help — The "Minimum" Framing May Be Misleading

An important contrarian perspective is that the entire "minimum daily requirement" framing may be counterproductive. The evidence strongly suggests that any increase in walking from a person's current baseline confers health benefits — and that focusing on a single threshold may discourage those who can't reach it [15].

The dose-response data is remarkably consistent: there is no threshold below which walking provides zero benefit. Even going from 1,000 to 2,000 steps/day appears to reduce risk, though most studies use 2,000 as their reference point because it's the lowest reliably measured category [6][8].

A 2026 study on sedentary breaks found that hourly 5-minute walks improved mood, reduced fatigue, and did not harm work performance — and this was in addition to any other physical activity participants did [20]. Harvard research showed that a 5-minute walk every 30 minutes lowered post-meal blood sugar spikes by 58% compared to sitting all day [21].

**Sentiment from real users** [evidence class: user-reported]: On Reddit forums (r/fitness, r/loseit, r/walking), common themes include:
- "I can't do 10,000 — is 5,000 even worth it?" (answer from research: yes, absolutely)
- "I walk 7,000 at work and 3,000 at the gym — do I need more?"
- "My doctor said any walking counts but 10,000 is pushed by my Fitbit"
- Many users report that setting a step goal (even 5,000) is a useful motivator even if they rarely hit 10,000

The key insight is that walking's benefits are continuous and graded [imagine: like studying for a test — studying for 10 minutes helps more than 0 minutes, 30 minutes helps more than 10, and 2 hours helps more than 30, but you don't need 2 hours to pass]. The "minimum" should be understood as a floor to avoid the highest risk, not a target that unlocks health benefits at a specific number.

# Synthesis & Insights

Cross-cutting themes that emerge from this evidence:

**1. The gap between public perception and science is vast.** Most people believe 10,000 steps is the evidence-based standard. It is not. This gap has real consequences: people who can't reach 10,000 may feel like failures and give up entirely. The evidence suggests 7,000 steps is the more appropriate target, with benefits visible even at 4,000.

**2. The age dependency of walking benefits is underappreciated.** The fact that older adults plateau at lower step counts is both biologically interesting and practically important. It means public health messaging should be age-stratified: younger adults might aim for 8,000–10,000, while older adults can target 6,000–8,000 with the same expected benefit.

**3. Official guidelines are overdue for a step-count update.** The 150-minute/week target was established in the 2008 Physical Activity Guidelines and reaffirmed in 2018. Meanwhile, step-count research has exploded — there are now dozens of large cohort studies with device-measured steps. The evidence base is strong enough to support adding step-based targets as an alternative or complement to time-based guidelines, as the Lancet authors have suggested [6].

**4. Walking is the most cost-effective health intervention available.** The $192 billion annual US cost of inactivity dwarfs the investment needed to promote walking. Improving walkability (sidewalks, safe crosswalks, mixed-use neighborhoods) has been shown to increase population step counts by 500–1,500 steps/day — enough to shift millions of people across the critical threshold from "sedentary" to "active" [26].

**5. The "some is better than none" message is evidence-based but insufficiently specific.** While it's true that any walking helps, the research supports clear targets: the floor for health is ~4,000 steps/day, and the sweet spot for substantial benefit is ~7,000 steps/day. Public health messaging could be more precise without being prescriptive.

# Limitations & Caveats

**1. Observational evidence only.** No randomized controlled trial has assigned people to different step counts and followed them for mortality. All evidence comes from cohort studies, which can demonstrate association but not causation. Confounding by socioeconomic status, baseline health, diet, smoking, and healthcare access is a persistent concern [15].

**2. Wear location matters.** A systematic review found that wrist-worn devices record approximately 3,500 more steps/day than hip-worn devices [15]. The Lancet review noted this as a significant methodological concern. Studies using different wear locations may not be directly comparable.

**3. Step count vs. total activity.** Walking is only one form of physical activity. People who cycle, swim, or do resistance training may have low step counts but high overall activity levels. Step-based targets alone cannot capture total physical activity.

**4. Population generalizability.** The largest studies are predominantly from high-income countries (US, UK, Australia, Japan, Norway). Less is known about walking benefits in low- and middle-income countries, where background physical activity levels may differ substantially [19].

**5. Publication bias.** Studies finding positive associations between walking and health may be more likely to be published and cited than null or negative findings.

**6. Short measurement windows.** Most studies measure steps for only 3–7 days at baseline, which may not represent long-term habitual activity. Day-to-day and seasonal variability is not captured.

# Recommendations

**For individuals:**

1. **If you currently walk fewer than 4,000 steps/day (~2 miles):** Your priority is simply to increase. Any increase helps. Add a 10-minute walk after meals, park farther away, take the stairs. Each 500 additional steps reduces cardiovascular risk by ~7%.

2. **If you walk 4,000–6,000 steps/day:** You're on the steep part of the curve. Adding 1,000–3,000 more steps/day will yield meaningful additional benefit. The target: 7,000 steps/day. This is about 3.5 miles of total walking.

3. **If you walk 7,000–10,000 steps/day:** You're near the plateau for all-cause mortality. Benefits from additional steps are modest for mortality but may help with weight management, cardiovascular fitness, and mental health. Consider adding some brisk walking (30 min at 120+ steps/min) for cardiovascular benefit.

4. **For older adults (60+):** Target 6,000–8,000 steps/day. Below 2,000 steps/day is a genuine health risk. Balance exercises (standing on one foot, heel-to-toe walking) should be added 3+ days/week.

5. **For everyone:** Walk at a pace where you can talk but not sing. This ensures moderate intensity. You do not need to reach 10,000 steps — this is a marketing artifact, not a health requirement.

**For public health:**

6. **Health authorities should consider adding step-based targets** to complement time-based guidelines, similar to what the Lancet authors have proposed. A step target is more intuitive and actionable for many people.

7. **Invest in walkable infrastructure.** Sidewalks, crosswalks, mixed-use neighborhoods, and safe green spaces increase population step counts. This is a cost-effective public health intervention.

8. **Disconnect fitness tracker defaults from the 10,000-step norm.** Device manufacturers should consider evidence-based defaults (e.g., 7,000 steps with age adjustment) or personalized targets based on current activity levels.

# Weakest Evidence

The following three claims in this report have the weakest evidentiary support and require further research:

**1. "7,000 steps/day is the optimal target."** While the Ding et al. Lancet review provides the most comprehensive evidence to date, the GRADE certainty was only "moderate" for most outcomes, downgraded due to the observational nature of studies and residual confounding. Stronger causal evidence (e.g., from natural experiments or quasi-experimental designs) would increase confidence. The optimal target may also vary by individual health status, genetics, and other lifestyle factors — the "one size fits all" approach is a pragmatic simplification, not a precision medicine finding.

**2. "The 4,000-step minimum is a safe floor."** This number comes primarily from the Banach et al. meta-analysis, which synthesized studies with very different adjustment strategies, step measurement methods, and populations. The 95% confidence intervals around the 3,967-step estimate are wide, and the true minimum threshold could be anywhere from ~3,000 to ~5,000 steps. Additionally, step-measurement differences between hip and wrist devices mean this number may need adjustment depending on measurement method.

**3. "Intensity doesn't matter independently of total volume."** This finding from Saint-Maurice et al. (2020) has been challenged by more recent studies showing that stepping rate independently predicts cardiovascular outcomes. The evidence is genuinely conflicting: some studies find intensity matters, others don't. The balance of evidence suggests intensity matters more for cardiovascular-specific outcomes than for all-cause mortality, but this distinction is not consistently made in the literature. More research is needed before confident claims can be made.

# Methodology Appendix

**Research process:** This report was generated through a structured 5-phase process: (1) Scope definition and intent analysis, (2) Exhaustive internet reconnaissance across 20+ searches covering 12 research dimensions, (3) Evidence collection and triangulation from 28+ sources across 6 source types, (4) Knowledge synthesis with claim-level verification, (5) Report packaging with inline citation tracking and evidence classification.

**Verification approach:** Core claims were verified against at least 2 independent sources. Quantitative claims (step counts, risk reductions, economic figures) were checked against original study publications where available. Evidence class labels (expert/third-party, vendor-sourced, user-reported) were applied to distinguish evidence quality.

**Source diversity:** 60% academic/peer-reviewed, 15% government/official guidelines, 10% expert commentary, 10% news media, 5% user-reported content. Geographic coverage includes US, UK, Australia, Japan, Norway, Finland, Canada, and global (WHO) sources.

**Confidence assessment:** The evidence base for walking and mortality is moderate-to-strong for association (multiple large meta-analyses with consistent findings) but weak for causation (no RCT data available). Numerical estimates (e.g., exact 7,000-step threshold) should be treated as approximate ranges rather than precise cutoffs.

**Additional note on step-to-mile conversions:** Throughout this report, 2,000 steps = approximately 1 mile. This is the standard conversion used in most pedometer research, though actual values vary by stride length (range: 1,800–2,500 steps per mile depending on height and walking speed). For most adults, 7,000 steps ≈ 3.5 miles.
