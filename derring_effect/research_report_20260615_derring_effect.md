# Research Report: The Derring Effect — Deliberate Errors Enhance Learning

## Executive Summary

- **What it is**: The derring effect (deliberately erring) is a counterintuitive learning phenomenon discovered by Dr. Sarah Shi Hui Wong and colleagues at the National University of Singapore / Singapore Management University. It shows that intentionally making and correcting conceptually plausible errors — even when you already know the correct answer — produces superior learning compared to traditional errorless study methods like copying, underlining, concept mapping, or generating correct elaborations [1][2][3].

- **Key findings across 6+ studies**: Deliberate erring improves (a) basic recall and retention [1], (b) higher-order knowledge application (analyzing novel scenarios) [2], (c) far transfer of concepts to remote, unrelated domains [3], (d) mathematical problem-solving [4], and (e) argumentative reasoning via "misteaching" [5]. It outperforms both passive study and active correct-elaboration strategies.

- **Why it works**: The effect is NOT fully explained by generation or elaboration benefits alone. Evidence points to enhanced target processing — the contrast between the deliberately wrong answer and the correct answer makes the correct information more distinctive and memorable [1][3]. Learners must personally generate (not just observe) their own errors.

- **Important caveat**: Two independent preregistered replication attempts by Mera et al. (2025) failed to reproduce the effect with British participants, suggesting possible cultural moderation or that the original effect sizes were inflated [6]. The original data from Wong's team is not publicly available [6].

- **How to apply**: Use deliberate erring AFTER initial instruction (you need to know the correct answer first). Generate a conceptually believable wrong answer (e.g., "bats are **birds** (mammals) that fly"), strike it out, and write the correct version. This works best in low-stakes practice contexts [7][8].

- **Learner blind spot**: Students consistently underestimate the technique's effectiveness even after experiencing better test performance on deliberately-erred material — so awareness alone isn't enough; structured implementation is key [1][2].

**Primary Recommendation**: For factual/definitional learning, adopt a 3-step study routine: (1) learn the material, (2) deliberately generate plausible-sounding wrong answers and correct them in writing, (3) test yourself. Reserve this for concepts where misconceptions commonly arise.

**Confidence Level**: Medium — the original findings are consistent across 6+ studies by the same lab, but independent replication has failed, and cultural generalizability is unestablished.

## Main Analysis

### Finding 1: The Derring Effect Is Robust Across 6+ Studies by Wong's Lab

In the original paper (Wong & Lim, 2022a, Journal of Experimental Psychology: General), three experiments showed that deliberately generating incorrect definitions for scientific concepts, then correcting them, outperformed errorless copying (Experiment 1), generating alternative correct answers / synonyms (Experiment 2), and generating real-world examples (Experiment 3) [1]. Effect sizes were substantial: for instance, the error-correction condition vs. copy condition yielded Cohen's d values around 0.43–0.80 depending on the comparison [6].

In Wong & Lim (2022b, Journal of Educational Psychology), two experiments (N=160) extended this to "meaningful learning" — not just recall but applying knowledge to analyze a novel news event. Deliberate erring beat both copying-with-underlining and concept mapping in both retention and application [2].

Wong (2023, Educational Psychology Review) showed deliberate erring improves far transfer — applying concepts to entirely different knowledge domains (e.g., from biology/vaccines to geography/forest management) [3]. This is the hardest form of learning to achieve.

Yap & Wong (2024, Journal of Educational Psychology) extended the finding to mathematical problem-solving: deliberately making and correcting errors in procedural practice improved transfer to more complex problems [4].

Most recently, Wong (2025) showed "learning by misteaching" — having students prepare to teach while deliberately including weak arguments — improved argumentative reasoning beyond normal teaching practice [5].

**Key Evidence**: Across all studies, the derring effect was consistently found. The mechanism appears to be "enhanced target processing" — generating an error makes the subsequent correction more salient and memorable. It is NOT reducible to generation, elaboration, or mere error exposure [1][3].

**Implications**: This is a genuinely new learning strategy, distinct from retrieval practice, elaborative interrogation, and productive failure. It requires knowing the correct answer first (unlike productive failure) [7].

**Sources**: [1], [2], [3], [4], [5]

### Finding 2: The Practical Method Is Simple but Specific

The technique in practice, from the original methods and practitioner guides [7][8][9]:

**Step 1**: Learn the material first. Read the textbook, listen to the lecture, watch the explanation. Deliberate erring is a **study/practice technique**, not a discovery-learning method [7].

**Step 2**: Generate a conceptually plausible error. For example, if learning "Bats are mammals that fly," write "Bats are **birds** (mammals) that fly." The error must be conceptually believable (a misunderstanding, not a typo) [1][7].

**Step 3**: Strike out the error and correct it. Physically cross it out (or use strikethrough) and write the correct version next to it [1].

The key principles are:
- **Personal generation**: You must create your own errors. Watching others' errors or correcting pre-made worksheets is less effective [3][8].
- **Conceptual plausibility**: Errors must be believable wrong answers (misconceptions), not random guesses. "Bats are humans that fly" is too implausible to be useful [1][7].
- **Low-stakes context**: Use this during practice and self-study, not graded assessment [8].
- **Correction is essential**: Making errors without correcting them is much less effective [1].

Psychology Today recommends comparing: use derring for one chapter and your usual method for another, then compare test results [9].

**Key Evidence**: The original methodology is precisely described in all papers. Practitioner guides from the Mathematics Hub (Australia) and Tips for Teachers (Craig Barton) distill these into classroom applications [7][8].

**Implications**: This is immediately actionable. No special materials needed — just pen, paper, and the willingness to be wrong on purpose.

**Sources**: [1], [7], [8], [9]

### Finding 3: Two Independent Replication Attempts Failed

Mera, Seabrooke, Higham, Thomas, and Modirrousta-Galian (2025, Journal of Experimental Psychology: General) published two preregistered replication attempts [6].

**Experiment 1** (lab, N~adequate): 36 term-definition concepts, within-subjects, error-correction vs. copy. No significant difference. Bayes factors supported the null hypothesis. [6]

**Experiment 2** (online): Trivia facts, similar protocol. Same null result. [6]

This is significant because it is the ONLY independent replication attempt published to date. The authors raised several concerns:

1. Wong's data is not publicly available (for any of the original studies) [6].
2. The original (2022a) excluded 10% of participants on non-preregistered grounds ("failed to conform") with no details [6].
3. Effect sizes in the original were surprisingly large (d up to 1.2 for some comparisons) [6].
4. All original studies used Singaporean students; the replication used British participants, suggesting a possible cultural boundary condition [6].

**Possible explanations**: (a) The derring effect is real but culturally specific — Asian educational cultures that emphasize error reflection may prepare students differently [6]. (b) The original findings are false positives or inflated by questionable research practices [6]. (c) Procedural differences in the replication created a conceptual (not direct) replication [6].

**Key Evidence**: The replication paper is published in the same journal as the original, peer-reviewed. It is transparent, preregistered, and uses adequate statistical power [6].

**Implications**: The derring effect should be treated as promising but not definitively established. Apply it if it works for you, but be aware the evidence base is from one lab.

**Sources**: [6]

### Finding 4: User Testimonies Are Anecdotal but Positive

I found no structured studies or large-scale surveys of real-world user experiences. Available testimonies come from blog posts and comments [10][11]:

Anil Mammen (edtech designer, substack post): Discusses implications for edtech — recommends designing practice environments where deliberate errors are rewarded, not penalized. If students think practice scores count toward grades, they won't make deliberate errors [10].

MickMel (personal blog): "I talk a lot about making mistakes... there is some evidence that intentionally making mistakes can be helpful too." Links to the Psychology Today article. No personal experience reported [11].

Craig Barton's "Tips for Teachers" podcast deep-dive (2024): The episode presents the research and suggests classroom applications but doesn't report student testimonials or outcome data from classroom use [8].

**This is a significant gap**: There are no published case studies, classroom implementation studies, or user surveys. The derring effect has been demonstrated only in controlled laboratory experiments with undergraduates studying scientific texts under time pressure. Real-world adoption is essentially zero in terms of documented evidence.

**Implications**: If you try this, document your results. You would be contributing original evidence.

### Finding 5: The Latest Development — "Misteaching" (Wong, 2025)

Wong's most recent paper (2025, Journal of Educational Psychology) investigates a combination of "learning by teaching" and deliberate erring called "misteaching" [5].

**Design**: 208 university students studied an argumentative text on a controversial topic using one of three methods: (a) notetaking, (b) correct teaching (prepare to teach, write a script with good arguments), or (c) misteaching (prepare to teach, but deliberately include weak/bad arguments for the audience to spot).

**Results**: Both teaching groups outperformed notetaking on recall. Misteaching produced ADDITIONAL gains on argumentative reasoning (weighing arguments, finding compromises) beyond correct teaching, even after controlling for recall. Students were unaware of this benefit [5].

This suggests deliberate erring can be layered on top of other active learning strategies (teaching) for higher-order outcomes.

**Key Evidence**: Single study, same lab, same limitations as other Wong studies [5].

**Implications**: For skills like reasoning, argumentation, and critical thinking, "misteaching" might be more powerful than simply studying errors or simply teaching correctly.

**Sources**: [5]

### Finding 6: The 3P Framework — How Errors Should Be Used Across Learning Stages

Wong & Lim (2019) developed the Prevention–Permission–Promotion (3P) framework for errors in education, which underpins the derring effect research [7]:

1. **Prevention**: Some errors should be prevented (safety-critical procedures, early skill acquisition).
2. **Permission**: Some errors should be allowed to occur naturally and discussed non-punitively.
3. **Promotion**: Some errors should be actively promoted — this is where deliberate erring fits. Guide learners to intentionally make and correct errors in low-stakes contexts.

This framework helps teachers decide WHEN to use deliberate erring vs. other approaches.

**Sources**: [7], [12]

## Synthesis & Insights

### Patterns Identified

**Pattern 1: The "correction contrast" mechanism unifies all findings.** Across recall, application, transfer, problem-solving, and reasoning, the common thread is that generating a wrong answer creates a contrast that makes the right answer more distinctive. This aligns with the broader cognitive psychology literature on the "hypercorrection effect" (high-confidence errors are especially well-corrected).

**Pattern 2: Personal generation is non-negotiable.** Passively seeing errors (others' mistakes, pre-made worksheets, generic corrections) does not produce the effect. The learner must actively construct the error and then actively correct it.

**Pattern 3: The effect size may be moderate or culturally bounded.** The failed replication suggests the original dramatic effects may not generalize. The most likely explanation is cultural — Singapore's education system emphasizes error analysis and correction more than Western systems, making students more adept at learning from deliberate errors.

### Novel Insights

**Insight 1: The derring effect is essentially "desirable difficulties" applied to errors.** Bjork's framework says that harder conditions during learning (spacing, interleaving, generation) produce better long-term retention. Deliberate erring is a particularly efficient desirable difficulty because the "difficulty" (wrong answer) directly highlights the target information.

**Insight 2: The metacognitive blind spot is itself a finding.** Across every single study, learners rated deliberate erring as less effective — even immediately after they scored higher on material they'd used it with. This means students will NOT adopt this strategy on their own, even if it works for them. Implementation requires external structure (teachers, study apps, curriculum design).

**Insight 3: Misteaching may be more scalable than individual deliberate erring.** In a classroom, having every student generate individual errors is time-intensive. But having students prepare to teach with deliberate errors (misteaching) achieves the same contrast benefit in a collaborative format.

### Implications

**For self-learners**: Try it for one topic and compare. Use it for concepts with common misconceptions. Keep a log to check if it actually helps you.

**For educators**: The 3P framework helps decide when to use this. Use deliberate erring during practice/review, not initial instruction. Be explicit about why — students won't trust the method otherwise.

**For researchers**: The failed replication demands attention. A large-scale, multi-lab replication with diverse cultural samples would resolve the controversy.

## Limitations & Caveats

### Counterevidence Register

**Contradictory Finding 1**: Two preregistered replications found no effect [6].
- Source: Mera et al. (2025), JEP: General
- Why it contradicts: Direct test of the same paradigm found null results
- How resolved: Possible cultural moderation or inflated original effects
- Impact on conclusions: Significant — reduces confidence from "high" to "medium"

**Contradictory Finding 2**: Original data is unavailable [6].
- Source: Observation by Mera et al.
- Why it contradicts: Open science norms require data sharing for verification
- Impact on conclusions: Moderate — limits ability to check original analyses

### Known Gaps

**Gap 1**: No classroom implementation studies. All studies are lab experiments with undergraduates. We don't know if this works with children, in real classrooms, or for subjects beyond science/definitions.

**Gap 2**: No longitudinal data. Effects measured immediately or after 2 days. No long-term retention data (weeks or months).

**Gap 3**: No user testimonial corpus. No blog posts, reviews, or case studies from actual users documenting personal results.

### Areas of Uncertainty

- Whether the derring effect generalizes beyond Singaporean undergraduates
- Optimal error plausibility (how wrong is too wrong / not wrong enough?)
- Dose-response: how many items should be studied with deliberate erring per session
- Whether the technique works for non-factual material (skills, procedures, creative domains)

## Recommendations

### Immediate Actions

1. **Try it for one study session.** Pick a topic with clear facts/definitions. Learn it normally for 10 min. Then for 10 min, write down deliberately wrong answers for key concepts, strike them out, and write the correct versions. Test yourself the next day.

2. **Compare it to your usual method.** Use deliberate erring for one chapter and your normal study method for another. Compare test scores. This is the Psychology Today recommendation and the best way to assess personal effectiveness.

3. **Document your results.** Write down whether it helped. This would contribute real-world evidence to a gap in the literature.

### Next Steps

- **Combine with teaching.** Try the "misteaching" variant — prepare to teach someone the material, but deliberately include wrong information for them to catch.
- **Use for misconceptions.** Identify concepts where you or others commonly make mistakes. These are ideal candidates for deliberate erring.
- **Explain the metacognitive blind spot to study partners.** Since learners consistently underestimate the technique, tell study buddies about the research so they persist with it.

### Further Research Needs

1. **Multi-lab replication** with diverse populations (Western, non-undergraduate, different ages)
2. **Classroom implementation studies** with real teachers and curricula
3. **Long-term retention data** (1 week, 1 month, 1 year)
4. **User-reported effectiveness** surveys or structured case studies
