# DEEP RESEARCH COMMISSION

## Research Question

What are the most effective natural remedies for treating an external hordeolum (stye) — a bacterial infection of an eyelash follicle on the lower eyelid near the lash line — and what is the quality of evidence supporting each remedy?

## Purpose & Context

A person has developed a painful boil-like lump on the right lower eyelid, on the outer side, near an eyelash. This is almost certainly an external hordeolum (stye) — an acute Staphylococcus aureus infection of a Zeis or Moll gland at the base of an eyelash follicle. They want to resolve it with natural/home remedies before considering medical intervention, and need to know which remedies actually work, which are dangerous, and when to abandon natural treatment and see a doctor.

This research serves a personal health decision: which natural remedy to apply, how to apply it safely, and what the red-line criteria are for seeking medical care.

### Audience

- **Primary**: Person with an active stye seeking safe, effective natural treatment
- **Secondary**: General readers interested in evidence-based natural eye care
- **Tone**: Precise, evidence-grounded, safety-conscious, accessible
- **Complexity**: Broad survey — needs to be understood by a layperson but backed by rigorous sourcing
- **Child-reader adaptivity**: Every medical term needs an inline [imagine: ...] explanation accessible to a 9-year-old

### Decision Context

- What remedy should the person start with RIGHT NOW (within the hour)?
- What's the daily treatment protocol?
- Which remedies are backed by evidence vs. folk tradition?
- What are the absolute contraindications and danger signs?
- When does "try natural" become "see a doctor"?

## Research Scope

### In Scope

1. External hordeolum (stye) — definition, causes, pathology, typical course
2. All natural/home remedies commonly recommended — warm compresses, tea bags (chamomile, green, black), tea tree oil, castor oil, coconut oil, manuka honey, aloe vera, turmeric, silver eye drops, gold ring, activated charcoal, garlic, herbal preparations
3. Evidence quality for each remedy — clinical trials, case reports, mechanistic rationale, traditional use
4. Safety profile of each remedy — risks, contraindications, proper application methods
5. Differential diagnosis — distinguishing stye from chalazion, blepharitis, preseptal cellulitis
6. Red flags and when to seek medical care

### Out of Scope

1. Prescription antibiotic treatments (except as comparison baseline)
2. Surgical treatment (incision and drainage)
3. Chronic or recurrent styes management
4. Cosmetic concerns or scar prevention
5. Eye conditions unrelated to eyelid infections

### Timeframe

Sources from 2000-2026, with emphasis on 2020-2026. Foundational medical knowledge (e.g., staphylococcal pathology) may use older authoritative sources.

### Geographic Focus

Global perspective, with emphasis on North American and European clinical guidelines.
