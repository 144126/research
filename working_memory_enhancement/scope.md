# Phase 1: SCOPE - Research Framing

## Research Question
What are the most effective evidence-based methods to improve short-term and working memory, spanning mental exercises, cognitive training protocols, thought experiments, metacognitive strategies, and physiological interventions (nutrition, exercise, sleep, supplements)?

## Core Components
1. Cognitive training protocols (dual n-back, complex span, CogMed, BrainHQ)
2. Mental/metacognitive strategies (method of loci, chunking, elaborative rehearsal, PQ4R, spaced retrieval)
3. Meditation/mindfulness (focused attention, open monitoring, MBSR)
4. Physiological interventions (aerobic exercise, resistance training, HIIT)
5. Nutrition/supplementation (omega-3, creatine, caffeine, magnesium, bacopa, flavonoids)
6. Sleep optimization (consolidation, naps, slow wave sleep)
7. Stress/cortisol management
8. Neurofeedback and brain stimulation (tDCS, TMS)

## Stakeholders
- Primary audience: Technically literate adult (50+) seeking actionable protocols
- Secondary: Researchers, clinicians, aging adults, students
- Evidence producers: Academic labs (Jaeggi, Buschkuehl, Sala, Gobet, Redick, Shipstead, Melby-Lervåg, Hulme, Klingberg), commercial entities (CogMed, BrainHQ/Posit Science), NIH-funded research
- Regulators: FTC (Lumosity action), FDA (supplement regulation)

## Scope Boundaries
**In scope:** Near/far transfer debate, dose-response relationships, mechanism explanations, effect sizes, practical protocols
**Out of scope:** Long-term/episodic memory, dementia/Alzheimer's treatment, prescription nootropics (modafinil, racetams), Baddeley model architecture, competitive memorization techniques

## Assumptions to Validate
1. Working memory training produces measurable improvements on trained tasks
2. Far transfer to fluid intelligence is possible but contested
3. Physiological interventions have stronger evidence than cognitive training for general cognitive enhancement
4. Individual differences (genetics, baseline capacity, age) moderate all intervention effects
5. Combination approaches are more effective than single interventions
