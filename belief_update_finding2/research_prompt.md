# DEEP RESEARCH COMMISSION

## Research Question
How can Finding 2 from the Contingent Belief Updating research — that hypothetical thinking drives the biasing effect of contingent belief updating — be applied as a self-administered technique for intentional belief change, without using any chemicals, drugs, or specialized equipment?

## Purpose & Context
The Contingent Belief Updating paper by Aina, Amelio, and Brütt (2023/2026) identifies three numbered findings about how contingent thinking distorts Bayesian belief updating. Finding 2 states: "Hypothetical thinking is driving the biasing effect of contingent belief updating." In the experimental context, this means that when people engage in hypothetical thinking (considering what they would believe if a yet-unobserved signal turned out one way vs. another), they deviate significantly from Bayesian rationality — more than when they simply update after observing actual evidence.

This research commission aims to invert Finding 2: rather than treating hypothetical thinking as a source of bias to be avoided, how can it be intentionally harnessed as a technique for self-directed belief change? The user specifically wants methods that require no chemicals (no psychedelics like psilocybin, LSD, or ketamine), no drugs, and no specialized equipment (no fMRI, EEG, TMS machines, or other hardware). The methods must be purely cognitive, behavioral, or conversational — doable alone or with another person using only mind and language.

The broader context spans: (a) the experimental economics literature on contingent belief updating and hypothetical thinking biases, (b) the clinical and self-help literature on belief change (CBT, REBT, memory reconsolidation, belief coding), (c) the rationalist/ LessWrong tradition of epistemic techniques (Crisis of Faith, Internal Double Crux, belief updating protocols), and (d) methods that specifically avoid the psychedelic-assisted therapy pathway (REBUS/REBAS model). The core tension is between viewing hypothetical thinking as a bias vs. viewing it as a lever.
