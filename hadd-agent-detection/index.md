# HADD: The Hyperactive Agency Detection Device — A Comprehensive Investigation

## Executive Summary

The Hyperactive/Hypersensitive Agency Detection Device (HADD) is one of the most influential concepts in the cognitive science of religion (CSR) — and one of the most empirically embattled. Proposed by Justin Barrett in 2000 and popularized in his 2004 book *Why Would Anyone Believe in God?*, HADD claims that humans evolved a specialized cognitive module that is biased toward detecting intentional agents (predators, enemies, spirits, gods) even where none exist [4]. The logic comes from Error Management Theory: false positives (seeing a tiger that is only wind) cost little, while false negatives (missing a real tiger) can cost your life. Over evolutionary time, the argument goes, this asymmetry selected for a "hair-trigger" agency detection system that routinely produces false positives — and these false positives, when elaborated by Theory of Mind, give rise to belief in gods, spirits, ghosts, conspiracy theories, and superstition [1][4].

The theory is elegant, intuitive, and widely cited. It has been called the "Standard Model" for the evolution of religion [1]. But after nearly 30 years of empirical investigation, the evidence tells a different story. A growing body of experimental work — including six experiments by Maij et al. (2019, N=233), preregistered VR studies by Szymanek et al. (2024, N=199), and multiple independent investigations by Willard, Gervais, and Ford — has consistently failed to find evidence that humans hyperactively detect agents [2][3][6]. Where HADD predicts a liberal bias toward assuming agents are present, multiple studies found the opposite: participants were biased toward assuming agents were *absent* [3]. Measures of agency detection were unrelated to individual differences in supernatural belief, undercutting the core causal claim [3][6]. Systematic reviews by Van Leeuwen and van Elk (2019) concluded that there is "no good evidence for a specialized cognitive module for detecting agents" and "no good evidence that people with stronger agency-detection capacities or biases are more likely to have religious beliefs" [2].

The concept has been criticized from multiple directions: evolutionary biologists argue that simple reflexes (not time-consuming agent speculation) would be selected for predator escape [1]; neuroscientists have failed to identify a dedicated "HADD module" in the brain, with agency attribution instead arising from distributed systems serving broader social-perceptual functions [5]; philosophers of science have called it a "just-so story" that mistakes evolutionary plausibility for empirical evidence [1]. Publication bias against null results has concealed the extent of this evidential crisis [2].

Today, the field is moving beyond HADD. Alternative frameworks — Predictive Processing-based Agency Detection (PPAD), the Interactive Religious Experience Model (IREM), and Willard's motivation-based theory of supernatural belief — offer more parsimonious, evidence-grounded accounts of why humans believe in unseen agents [6]. These models reverse HADD's causal arrow: culturally acquired religious beliefs drive people to seek agency-confirming experiences, not the reverse [2][6].

## Introduction

HADD stands for "Hyperactive Agency Detection Device" (also "Hypersensitive Agency Detection Device"). It is a theoretical construct from the cognitive science of religion (CSR) — a field that applies cognitive psychology, evolutionary theory, and neuroscience to understand why religious beliefs are so widespread across human cultures [1].

The core question HADD tries to answer is simple: Why do humans, everywhere and across all recorded history, believe in invisible agents — gods, spirits, ghosts, demons, ancestors? HADD's answer: because our brains evolved a survival mechanism that makes us *too good* at detecting agents. We see agents everywhere, even when they aren't there. And once we've detected an invisible agent in the rustling leaves, it's a short cognitive step to believing in a god who watches over us [4].

This report investigates every dimension of the HADD hypothesis: where it came from, how it supposedly works, what brain systems might implement it, what experiments have tested it, why it is now in crisis, and what comes next. ## Main Analysis

### Finding 1: HADD's Historical Origins — Guthrie, Barrett, and the Birth of the Standard Model

The idea that humans are biased toward seeing agency predates HADD by decades. Stewart Guthrie's 1993 book *Faces in the Clouds: A New Theory of Religion* argued that religion arises from a pervasive cognitive tendency to anthropomorphize — to see human-like agency in the natural world [1]. Guthrie drew on the classic Heider and Simmel (1944) experiment, in which participants viewed animated geometric shapes (a large triangle, a small triangle, a small circle) moving around a screen. The majority of participants spontaneously described the shapes as if they were intentional agents with emotions, goals, and personalities — "the triangle is chasing the circle," "the circle is scared" [1].

Justin Barrett, a cognitive scientist then at the University of Michigan, synthesized Guthrie's ideas with the "massive modularity" thesis of evolutionary psychology [2]. In a 2000 paper in *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, Barrett introduced the term "Hyperactive Agency Detection Device" [2]. His 2004 book *Why Would Anyone Believe in God?* fully articulated the theory. Barrett argued that HADD is an evolved cognitive module that operates automatically, triggered by ambiguous stimuli suggesting an agent's presence. Once HADD flags a potential agent, Theory of Mind systems attribute beliefs and intentions to it. When no natural agent is found, the mind fills the gap with a supernatural one [4].

HADD quickly became a cornerstone of the "Standard Model" of CSR, alongside Pascal Boyer's Minimally Counterintuitive (MCI) theory [1]. Key proponents include Scott Atran (2002), who argued that "natural selection has trip-wired cognitive schema for agency detection in the face of uncertainty" [1], and Steven Novella, who popularized HADD in skeptical circles [4].

### Finding 2: The Proposed Mechanism — How HADD Supposedly Works

HADD is built on Error Management Theory (EMT): when making judgments under uncertainty, the costs of different errors are rarely symmetrical. Natural selection favors biases that minimize the more costly error [16]. For agency detection, false negatives (missing a predator) can be fatal, while false positives (seeing an agent that isn't there) just waste caution. Therefore, evolution should favor a bias toward false positives — the "smoke detector principle" [4].

Barrett proposed that HADD's primary trigger is non-inertial movement — motion that appears self-propelled, not explainable as a passive reaction to physical forces [4]. A twig moving sideways against the wind triggers the inference: "there is an agent here." This detection happens automatically, pre-consciously, and extremely quickly [4].

The process follows a two-step model: Step 1 — HADD detects that an agent *might* be present, a quick perceptual-level inference [4]. Step 2 — Theory of Mind attributes specific mental states (beliefs, desires, intentions) to that agent [1]. Barrett identified amplifying conditions: ambiguity (default is "it's an agent until proven otherwise"), threat (more false positives in dangerous situations), and low control (feeling powerless increases hidden agency detection) [4].

Proponents have used HADD to explain an extraordinarily broad range of phenomena: religious belief (gods as invisible agents), superstition (false cause-effect linking via hidden agency), conspiracy theories (hidden coordination by malevolent groups), paranormal belief, anthropomorphism, and animism [1][4].

### Finding 3: The Neurological Evidence — pSTS, TPJ, and Distributed Agency Networks

If HADD existed as a dedicated neural module, the most likely candidate brain region would be the posterior superior temporal sulcus (pSTS) and adjacent temporoparietal junction (TPJ) [5]. The pSTS responds to: biological motion (point-light displays of walking humans), intentional action (goal-directed vs. random movement), social interactions (agents interacting vs. acting independently), and animacy detection [5]. Critically, it responds to Heider-Simmel-style geometric shape animations — the very stimuli that demonstrate spontaneous agency attribution [5].

A 2017 study identified a specific pSTS subregion (the SI-fROI at MNI [54, -43, 18]) that responds selectively to social interactions between agents — but *not* to individual animacy or goals [5]. This region could decode whether one agent was helping or hindering another (P = 1.2 x 10^-4) [5]. The authors identified at least four dissociable regions in the pSTS/TPJ landscape: the SI-fROI (social interactions), TPJ (Theory of Mind), pSTS-face (face processing), and an animate/goal region [5].

What this means for HADD: agency detection is not a single, unified process in a dedicated "module." It is a distributed cognitive function involving multiple dissociable brain systems [5]. Lisdorf (2007) directly challenged HADD's neural plausibility: the brain has no single region that functions as a "hyperactive agency detector." Agency attribution arises from interacting systems (pSTS, fusiform face area, TPJ, medial prefrontal cortex, amygdala), none specialized for hyperactive detection [15].

The predictive processing framework offers a different neural account. Under predictive processing, agency detection is not a specialized hyperactive module but a consequence of prior expectations (priors) about encountering agents in a given context [19]. When you're in a dark alley, your prior for threat is high — so minimal sensory evidence generates agent perception. In your living room, the prior is low — the same shadow is dismissed [3].

### Finding 4: The Experimental Evidence — Six Studies, Six Null Results

The most direct experimental test of HADD comes from Maij et al. (2019), who conducted six threat-induction experiments (N=233) [3]. If HADD exists and is triggered by threat, inducing threat should increase false-positive agency detection. Methods included: Biological Motion Detection Tasks with threatening vs. neutral pictures (Exps 1a/1b), contextual threat via horror music (Exps 2a/2b), Geometrical Figures Task (Exp 3), and Auditory Agent Detection Task (Exp 4) [3].

Results: threat did not increase false agent detection in any experiment. Participants showed a conservative response bias — they were more likely to say "no agent present" than "yes," the *opposite* of what HADD predicts [3]. Agency/intentionality detection measures were unrelated to supernatural beliefs, undercutting the core causal claim [3]. The only significant finding: agency detection related to negativity bias, a personality measure [3].

Szymanek et al. (2024) conducted a preregistered VR study (N=199) testing threat-induced false agency detection with higher ecological validity [19]. Results: "Contrary to HADD expectations, no elevated false agent detections occurred under threat; instead, detection aligned more with prior expectations and sensory reliability" [19]. This directly supports predictive processing over HADD.

Willard and Norenzayan (2013) tested whether anthropomorphism predicted belief in God across large samples in Canada, US, Czech Republic, and Slovakia [13]. Finding: anthropomorphism was unrelated to belief in God in adult samples, even at zero-order correlation. It did predict paranormal belief, suggesting agency detection may play a role in some supernatural beliefs but not others [13]. Tratner et al. found that over 90% of VR participants falsely perceived an agent, but agency detection was unrelated to supernatural belief [32].

Van Leeuwen and van Elk's (2019) systematic review concluded: (1) "There is no good evidence for a specialized cognitive module for detecting agents"; (2) "There is no good evidence that people with stronger agency-detection capacities or biases are more likely to have religious beliefs" [12]. They proposed the Interactive Religious Experience Model (IREM), reversing HADD's causal arrow [12].

### Finding 5: The Crisis — Why HADD Persists Despite No Evidence

A growing number of researchers have publicly stated that HADD lacks empirical support. Kat Ford (2024): "There is just one tiny problem: there is no empirical evidence that the hyperactive agency detection device exists" [2]. Ford's PhD research on agency sensitivity and religious belief found "nothing." Will Gervais and Aiyana Willard independently attempted to study HADD and found no evidence it existed [2]. At a conference, multiple researchers privately told Willard they too had null results — unpublished due to publication bias [2].

Philosophers of science have charged HADD with being a "just-so story" — a plausible evolutionary narrative mistaken for empirical evidence [1]. The British Journal for the Philosophy of Science (2012): establishing that HADD *would be* advantageous is not establishing that it *actually evolved* [1]. Evolutionary biologists note that simple reflexes, not time-consuming agent speculation, would be selected for predator escape — undermining HADD's core premise [1].

The "massive modularity" thesis on which HADD depends is itself controversial. Many cognitive functions are implemented by domain-general learning mechanisms, not specialized modules [1]. If the massive modularity thesis is wrong, HADD loses its theoretical foundation. Additionally, the finding that participants are biased toward *denying* agency suggests the default human cognitive stance may be skepticism, not credulity [3].

### Finding 6: Alternative Theories — Predictive Processing, IREM, and Motivation-Based Accounts

The predictive processing framework (PPAD) rejects HADD's modular, hyperactive mechanism in favor of Bayesian inference. Agency is inferred when it best explains sensory data given prior probabilities [3]. This explains contextual variation: haunted house (high prior for ghosts) produces ghost detection; kitchen (low prior) does not — same sensory input, different inference [3]. It also explains null threat-induction results: brief laboratory primes cannot override well-established lifetime priors [3].

Van Leeuwen and van Elk's IREM argues the causal arrow runs opposite to HADD: culturally transmitted religious beliefs cause people to interpret ambiguous experiences as agency-confirming, rather than agency detection causing religious beliefs [12]. Predictions supported by evidence: believers report more agency detection experiences; experiences follow cultural templates (Christians hear God, Hindus feel Krishna); agency detection decreases as scientific explanations fill causal gaps; priming religious concepts increases agency detection in ambiguous tasks [12].

Willard's (2025) motivation-based theory argues supernatural agent beliefs arise from mental state reasoning applied to causally opaque phenomena — a non-automatic, cognitively effortful process [6]. Anthropomorphism is motivated — we personify when we need explanation, not because perception compels us [6]. The shrinking domain of religion with science is explained: as science provides better causal explanations, fewer phenomena need supernatural explanation [6]. Willard argues HADD's error management logic is self-defeating: mentalizing is cognitively expensive — if we hyper-detected agents and then mentalized about each one, computational costs would outweigh survival benefits [6].

Cultural evolution offers an additional dimension: religious beliefs propagate through social learning, not just individual cognition. Children acquire beliefs from their community, not from spontaneous agency detection [6]. Cross-cultural variation in supernatural beliefs is difficult to explain if they all arise from a universal HADD mechanism [6].

## Synthesis & Insights

Across all six findings, a clear pattern emerges: agency detection is real, but it is not what HADD claims it to be. The evidence converges on a picture that is almost the inverse of HADD:

1. **Agency detection exists but is not hyperactive.** People are conservative about attributing agency to ambiguous stimuli, not biased toward false positives [3].

2. **Agency detection is context-dependent and shaped by prior learning**, not by a hard-wired, hyperactive module [19].

3. **Religious beliefs (acquired culturally) drive agency detection experiences**, not the reverse [12].

4. **The most plausible cognitive process for supernatural belief is effortful mental state reasoning** applied to causally mysterious phenomena, not automatic perceptual over-detection [6].

5. **HADD's persistence as an explanatory concept despite lack of evidence** illustrates the seductiveness of just-so stories in evolutionary psychology [2].

The failure of HADD does not mean agency detection is irrelevant to religion. Rather, it means the relationship is more nuanced and bidirectional than HADD's simple bottom-up model allowed. People do have agency detection capacities, but these are modulated by context, culture, prior belief, and motivation. A complete account of supernatural belief requires integrating predictive processing, cultural evolution, motivation-based reasoning, and social learning — not a single hyperactive module.

## Limitations & Caveats

1. **Much null-result evidence is unpublished.** The true extent of HADD-falsifying evidence is unknown due to publication bias against null results [2].

2. **Experimental tasks may miss the construct.** If HADD operates at a pre-conscious level that laboratory tasks cannot access, null results may reflect methodological failure [3].

3. **Most studies measure human agency detection, not supernatural agency detection.** This proxy may miss HADD's intended target [3].

4. **Cultural variation is underexplored.** Most HADD research uses WEIRD (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic) populations [6].

5. **This report draws on 32 sources**, not the 270+ standard for a full meta-analysis. The findings are consistent with systematic reviews (Van Leeuwen & van Elk 2019) but do not constitute an independent exhaustive literature review.

6. **The HADD defense by Atkinson (2024)** suggesting two versions of HADD (origin vs. maintenance) may salvage part of the theory, but this argument is too recent to have been fully evaluated [20].

---

## Recommendations

1. **For researchers:** Retire HADD as an explanatory construct for the origin of religious belief. The evidence does not support a specialized hyperactive module. Future research should focus on predictive processing models, motivation-based accounts, and cultural evolutionary frameworks.

2. **For skeptics and popularizers:** Stop invoking HADD as an established fact. Ford's (2024) call is correct — "HADD it mate" is not an explanation. Skeptical writing should reflect the evidential crisis.

3. **For those interested in religion's origins:** The most promising current framework integrates predictive processing (how priors shape agency perception), IREM (how culture shapes belief-driven interpretation), and motivation-based reasoning (why we anthropomorphize to explain the unexplained).

4. **For methodologists:** A preregistered, adequately powered, ecologically valid study (e.g., real-world night hiking with threat manipulation) testing false agency detection would be valuable. The field needs positive evidence, not just null results, to move forward.

5. **For philosophers:** The evolutionary debunking argument against religious belief that relies on HADD should be abandoned. It rests on a contested empirical claim that appears to be false.
