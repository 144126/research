## 2. Central Thesis

> "There is no empirical evidence that the hyperactive agency detection device exists."

The author argues that HADD — despite being widely invoked in the cognitive science of religion (CSR) to explain belief in gods, ghosts, conspiracies, aliens, etc. — lacks any empirical support as an inherited, evolved cognitive mechanism. The article proposes that agency detection is better explained by **cultural learning and context-dependent expectations** rather than an innate hyperactive module.

## 5. Verification Appendix — External Evidence

### 5.1 Systematic Reviews and Meta-Studies

**Van Leeuwen, N. & van Elk, M. (2019). "Seeking the Supernatural: The Interactive Religious Experience Model." *Religion, Brain & Behavior*, 9(3), 221-275.**
- **Key finding**: "HADD Theory...presently has two main empirical problems: (1) there is no good evidence for a specialized cognitive module for detecting agents...and (2) there is no good evidence that people with stronger agency-detection capacities or biases are more likely to have religious beliefs."
- **Proposed alternative**: Interactive Religious Experience Model (IREM) — general belief in supernatural agents (acquired culturally) *causes* people to seek situations that trigger agency-detection experiences, not the reverse.
- **This is a major peer-reviewed target article with commentaries** — confirms the article's central thesis.

### 5.2 Experimental Studies

**Maij, D. L. R., et al. (2019). "The boundary conditions of the hypersensitive agency detection device." *Religion, Brain & Behavior*.**
- **Method**: 6 threat-inducing experiments, N = 233
- **Tasks**: Biological Motion Detection Task (×2), Geometrical Figures Task, Auditory Agent Detection Task, plus VR horror environment
- **Results**: Threat did NOT increase false agent detection. Participants showed a **response bias toward assuming agents were ABSENT** (opposite of HADD prediction).
- **Relation to supernatural beliefs**: Agency/intentionality detection measures were **unrelated** to individual differences in supernatural beliefs.
- **This directly contradicts HADD predictions.**

**Tratner, A. E., et al. "Fear the Unseen: Supernatural Belief and Agency Detection in Virtual Reality." *Evolutionary Behavioral Sciences*.**
- **Method**: VR horror environment
- **Results**: Over 90% of participants falsely perceived an agent at least once. However, agency detection was **unrelated** to supernatural agent beliefs.
- **Conclusion**: "Our results failed to support this hypothesis because they did not indicate an association between agency detection and belief in supernatural agents."

**Willard, A. K. & Norenzayan, A. (2013). "Cognitive biases explain religious belief, paranormal belief, and belief in life's purpose." *Cognition*, 129(2), 379-391.**
- **Method**: Large samples from Canada, US, Czech Republic, Slovakia
- **Results**: Anthropomorphism (operationalized HADD analog) was **not related to belief in God** in the adult sample, even at zero-order correlation. It did predict paranormal belief.
- **Interpretation**: In Christian-majority cultures, believers may **suppress** anthropomorphism (it's considered idolatry).

### 5.3 Theoretical Critiques

**Willard, A. K. (2024/2025). "Belief as explanation: a motivation-based theory of agency and anthropomorphism in religious belief." Brunel University Research Archive.**
- **Key claims**: "The most famous of these [theories], the Hyperactive Agency Detection Device (HADD), has garnered several strong critiques and **no supportive empirical evidence in almost 30 years**."
- **Alternative**: Supernatural agent creation comes from "the use of mental state reasoning to explain phenomenon beyond our current causal understanding" — a **non-automatic, cognitively effortful process**.
- **Explains**: The shrinking domain of religion with the growth of science, and connection between origins of supernatural beliefs and cultural evolution of religions.
