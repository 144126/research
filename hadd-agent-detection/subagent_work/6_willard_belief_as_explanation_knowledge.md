# Willard & Russell-Wilks (2025) — Belief as Explanation

**Source:** Willard, A.K. & Russell-Wilks, C. (2025). Belief as explanation: a motivation-based theory of agency and anthropomorphism in religious belief. *Religion, Brain & Behavior*, 0(0), 1–15. doi:10.1080/2153599X.2025.2584792

**URL:** https://bura.brunel.ac.uk/bitstream/2438/32248/6/FullText.pdf

**Status:** Open Access (CC BY 4.0). Published 27 Nov 2025. Funded by John Templeton Foundation (Grant 61928).

## Core Thesis

Willard & Russell-Wilks argue that HADD — the most famous theory in the cognitive science of religion for why supernatural agent beliefs are ubiquitous — has "no supportive empirical evidence in almost 30 years" and propose a **motivation-based alternative**: the tendency to create supernatural agents comes from using mental state reasoning to explain phenomena beyond our current causal understanding, as a **non-automatic, cognitively effortful process**, not from automatic perceptual over-detection of agents.

They replace "a bias towards agency detection with a bias towards explanations, which sometimes engages mental states (and thus minds) as these explanations."

This perspective claims to account for two underspecified problems in the literature: (1) the shrinking domain of religion with the growth of science, and (2) connecting the origin of supernatural beliefs to the cultural evolution of religions.

## PART 1: CRITIQUES OF HADD

### 1.1 No Empirical Evidence (Core Claim)

> "Despite it originating in the late 90s, allowing for plenty of time to do the research, there has never been any compelling empirical evidence for a tendency to hyperactively detect agents and a substantial number of null findings suggesting we have no bias towards preferentially detecting agents at all."

This is the paper's central empirical claim against HADD. They cite Van Leeuwen & Van Elk (2019) as providing the best overview of this lack of evidence.

### 1.2 No Bias Toward Agent Detection — Reverse Pattern

> "If anything, research has shown a reluctance to detect agents; people are more prone to assume the absence of agents when cues are ambiguous rather than the other way around."

Cites Maij et al. (2019) as evidence that the bias runs opposite to HADD predictions.

### 1.3 Direction of Causality Is Reversed

> "In research that has found links between agency detection and some forms of supernatural belief (Van Elk, 2013), further evidence has suggested that this relationship is driven by religious belief—people are detecting agents because they believe in them, rather than the other way around."

Cites Andersen et al. (2019); Szymanek et al. (2024); Van Elk et al. (2016). This directly inverts HADD's causal logic.

### 1.4 Mistaking Human Form ≠ Mistaking Human Mind

> "Mistakenly seeing human forms and human minds are distinct (though related) processes, and it is not clear that seeing something that looks like a person necessarily leads to, or is necessary for, reasoning about something as though it has a mind."

This is a critical theoretical distinction: Guthrie's anthropomorphism (perceptual — faces in clouds) is conflated with the mental-state attribution required for religious belief. The paper notes that people give human-like agency to things like weather and nature that have **no physical characteristics of an agent at all**, suggesting the process is not perceptually driven.

### 1.5 Face Pareidolia Is Quickly Corrected and Doesn't Imply Agency

> "Face detection in the absence of other cues does not lead to the belief that things are alive or have agency."

Pareidolia is corrected in ~250ms (Wardle et al., 2020). Biases in interpreting faces (e.g., seeing Jesus in toast) are **learned cognitive biases, not perceptual ones** (Wardle et al., 2022). Even young children know dolls are not alive despite looking human (Legerstee et al., 1987).

### 1.6 Multiple Cues Required — Not "Hyperactive"

> "Multiple cues of agency are necessary before an agent is detected."

The paper notes that Heider & Simmel's (1944) geometric shapes were designed to move like agents, and participants' interpretations were metaphorical. The participants "did not believe the shapes on the screen were truly alive and in love."

### 1.7 Mental State Attribution Is Cognitively Costly (Incompatible with HADD Logic)

> "Even if an 'agency detection device' is hyperactive (and the evidence suggests it is not), the over-detection of agents would only be a cost-effective mechanism if we don't elaborate on the mental states of those agents."

The paper argues that:
- Thinking about mental states is cognitively effortful (Apperly & Butterfill, 2009)
- If HADD triggered fight-or-flight, mental state attribution would be unnecessary — you should just flee
- Yet religious belief requires rich mental state reasoning about gods' thoughts, desires, etc.

### 1.8 Supernatural Agents Often Lack Bodies for Perceptual Detection

> "Many supernatural agents don't have bodies, or physical presences at all, to be mistaken for perceptually cued agency (gods aren't frequently believed to be lurking in the bushes)."

Even when agency is detected through movement, "assigning a mind full of thoughts and beliefs is not an automatic or costless process."

### 1.9 Error Management Logic Is Questionable

The paper notes that the error management reasoning for threat detection "has been questioned; see McKay & Efferson, 2010," though they don't elaborate extensively on this.

### 1.10 HADD Doesn't Explain Two Key Phenomena

HADD fails to account for:
1. **The shrinking domain of religion** — why belief declines where scientific explanations succeed
2. **Cultural evolution of religion** — how individual agency detection connects to complex religious institutions

### 1.11 Rebuttal of Atkinson (2024) Defense

Atkinson (2024) argued that HADD has been used in two ways (origin vs. maintenance) and that confusion between these led to mistaken rejection. Willard & Russell-Wilks counter:

> "We read this differently. Researchers seem well aware of the perspective of HADD as the origin of religion, but have looked at HADD as a way of maintaining religion as an attempt to salvage the theory in light of a lack of evidence for any positive bias in agency detection."

They note that Barrett (2004) himself used HADD in both ways.

### 1.12 Modernized Agency Detection Theories Remove Causal Role

Van Leeuwen & Van Elk's (2019) Interactive Religious Experience Model and Andersen's (2017) predictive coding account "make agency detection a consequence of belief," removing it "as a causal factor in the origin of supernatural belief."

## PART 2: THE MOTIVATION-BASED ALTERNATIVE THEORY

### 2.1 Core Mechanism

> "We are proposing that the tendency to use theory of mind on things that are not human minds arises from our curiosity and need for explanation rather than as a heuristic reaction to detect agents from perceptual cues."

The theory replaces a **perceptual bias** with an **explanatory bias**:
- Humans have a preference for finding any explanation rather than leaving things unexplained (Keil, 2006)
- When simpler causal mechanisms are unavailable, we reach for social explanations about other minds (Epley et al., 2007)
- Mental state explanations fill gaps when important things remain unexplained

### 2.2 Three Motivations for Anthropomorphism (from Epley et al., 2007)

The paper adopts the three-factor theory of anthropomorphism:
1. **Ease of explanation** — explaining things with familiar human thoughts and desires is cognitively accessible
2. **Effectance motivation** — the need to understand and potentially influence things; minds are something we can influence
3. **Social connection** — coping with loneliness (less relevant for most supernatural beliefs)

The first two are identified as most relevant for supernatural agent beliefs.

### 2.3 It Is a Cognitively Effortful Process

> "We borrow from Tanya Luhrmann (2012, 2020) in suggesting that this type of reasoning about supernatural minds is an effortful process, rather than one that is low cost and automatic."

Reasoning about supernatural minds is "cultivated and practiced" — something that requires learning and makes the experience stronger over time. A "similar slow and rationalized process" is used to create new supernatural agent beliefs.

### 2.4 Example: Weather as Explanation

Difficult-to-predict but important phenomena (like weather) commonly receive supernatural explanations worldwide (Jackson et al., 2023). The paper argues people are most motivated to explain weather supernaturally when it behaves anomalously — e.g., a bad storm caused by anger.

This not only provides an explanation but offers a sense of control: if the storm is caused by anger, you can appease the agent behind it.

### 2.5 Associative Learning as Evolutionary Basis

> "The ineffectiveness of these actions is extremely difficult to evidence outside of a scientific framework (a technology not present for much of human history), and random association between the proposed cause and effect may be enough to support beliefs."

Superstitious behavior is an "inevitable outcome of an optimal associative learning strategy" (Abbott & Sherratt, 2011; Beck & Forstmeier, 2007). Trying something is better than trying nothing when stakes are high (Foster & Kokko, 2008). This is phylogenetically deep, observed from Skinner's (1948) pigeons upward.

### 2.6 Connection to Cultural Evolution

> "A simple agentic explanation for something unpredictable like anomalous weather can be elaborated and expanded through generations to create the complex belief systems we see in the world today."

Examples provided:
- Balinese religious beliefs supporting effective rice farming (Lansing, 2012)
- Mentawai water spirit Sikameinan enforcing meat sharing norms (Singh et al., 2020)
- Witchcraft beliefs enforcing norms against envy in Mauritius (Willard et al., 2025)

## PART 3: MENTAL STATE REASONING ACCOUNT

### 3.1 Mentalizing and God Belief — Weak and Unreliable Link

> "Research into the relationship between mentalizing and God beliefs has demonstrated an unreliable and weak link between increased mentalizing abilities and greater belief in supernatural agents."

Cites Lindeman et al. (2015); Willard & Norenzayan (2013). The paper argues we should not expect strong effects: "A capacity does not necessarily lead to a tendency."

### 3.2 Autism and Religious Belief

> "More recent evidence has found no difference in belief in gods between those with autism and those who are not."

The classic claim (Norenzayan et al., 2012) that mentalizing deficits in autism explain lower religious belief is challenged. Reddish et al. (2016) found no difference. People with autism "believe in a variety of supernatural agents, and have personal relationships with them," and show "high levels of anthropomorphism" (Clutterbuck et al., 2022; Visuri, 2012, 2018).

> "These tendencies to reason about supernatural minds are not contingent on the ability to read emotions and other mental states in physical cues such as facial expressions and body language."

### 3.3 Cultural Variation in Mind Concepts

> "Different cultures have different notions about the mind, and thus the supernatural minds they create to explain the world are likely to reflect the specific folk psychology of a particular cultural milieu."

When new types of supernatural minds are introduced (e.g., Christian omniscient God into a society that holds minds unknowable), people can update their concepts (McNamara et al., 2021). This demonstrates that thinking about supernatural minds is "a careful rationalized process that requires learning rather than an automatic one."

### 3.4 Capacity ≠ Tendency

A crucial theoretical point: having the capacity for mental state reasoning does not entail a tendency to use it for supernatural belief. This undermines simple linear models where more mentalizing = more religious belief.

## PART 4: EVIDENCE FOR CULTURAL LEARNING

### 4.1 Belief Decline with Scientific Explanations

> "The proposal that we create and use supernatural agent beliefs as explanations for difficult to explain phenomena fits better with patterns of belief decline in areas where more successful scientific explanations become available."

The paper argues this is not about disproving beliefs but replacing them. Rainmaking rituals in China declined not because they were demonstrated ineffective but because people's worldview changed through exposure to different perspectives (Hong et al., 2023). Similar accounts for astrology and alchemy in Europe (Clements, 2017; Kemp, 2003).

### 4.2 Explanatory Pluralism

> "By nature humans are explanatory pluralists (Lombrozo, 2010). We look for, and are willing to accept, multiple types of explanations, often at the same time."

Different types of questions ("why me, why this, why now?") require different evidence (Davoodi & Lombrozo, 2022; Metz et al., 2023). Mechanistic answers (germ theory) don't satisfy existential questions, so supernatural explanations persist alongside scientific ones.

### 4.3 Classic Anthropological Evidence

- **Evans-Pritchard (1937) on Azande witchcraft**: The Azande knew termites caused the granary collapse, but witchcraft explained *why it fell on those specific people at that specific time*
- **Malinowski (1922, 1954) on Argonauts**: Religious rituals were response to uncertainty — more ritual engagement in deep-sea fishing (high uncertainty) than lagoon fishing (low uncertainty)

### 4.4 Social Transmission and Elaboration

> "Explanations can be socially transmitted and become subject to the cultural evolutionary pressures that can lead to the formation of a religion."

The "explanation explanation" offers a clear path from individual belief creation to religiosity — explanations are shared, elaborated, and refined across generations.

## PART 5: PAPER LIMITATIONS AND NUANCES

### 5.1 Role of Agency Detection Not Entirely Rejected

> "The detection of goal directed behavior, or intentionality, likely still plays a role in our motivation to attribute minds to the world."

People are more likely to give minds to things that cue agency (dogs > cars), "but even when agency detection increases the chance of attributing mental states, the response is still a motivated one — there is something you want to explain."

### 5.2 Not a New Idea

The paper acknowledges: "The idea that religion, and agency in religion, is used as explanation is in no way a new idea (e.g., Hume, 1779; Tylor, 1871)." The novelty is in the psychological investigation and integration with evidence.

### 5.3 No Direct Empirical Test

The paper is a theoretical review and synthesis — it does not present new empirical data directly testing the motivation-based theory against HADD.

### 5.4 Self-Citation Considerations

Aiyana Willard is a prominent critic of HADD and has published null findings on the topic previously. Her own prior work is cited heavily. This is appropriate but the critical perspective should be noted.

## Verification from External Sources

1. **The Skeptic (Ford, Nov 2024):** Kat Ford (PhD, Brunel) independently reports that during her PhD she "tried to investigate a link between heightened sensitivity to agents and religious belief and found… nothing." Willard and Gervais also "found no evidence that HADD even existed." Multiple other researchers reported similar null results.

2. **Szymanek et al. (2025) VR study:** Preregistered VR study of 199 participants found that threat did *not* increase false agency detection, "which casts doubt on HADD conceptualized as a threat-triggered mechanism." Published in *Psychology of Religion and Spirituality*.

3. **Deymond Laplasa analysis (Feb 2026):** Rates HADD claim as "Unproven" (L2 evidence level). Notes "direct evidence for a specialized HADD 'device' remains limited" and "alternative explanations through predictive processing are gaining increasing support." Confirms the modularity vs. general processes debate.
