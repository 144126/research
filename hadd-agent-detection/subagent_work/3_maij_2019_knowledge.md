# SOURCE ANALYSIS: Maij et al. (2019) — Boundary Conditions of HADD

## Source Metadata
- **Title**: The boundary conditions of the hypersensitive agency detection device: an empirical investigation of agency detection in threatening situations
- **Authors**: David L. R. Maij, Hein T. van Schie, Michiel van Elk
- **Journal**: Religion, Brain & Behavior, 2019, Vol. 9, No. 1, pp. 23–51
- **DOI**: 10.1080/2153599X.2017.1362662
- **Affiliations**: a) University of Amsterdam; b) Radboud University Nijmegen
- **License**: CC BY-NC-ND
- **Access**: Open access via UvA-DARE / Radboud Repository

## Study Design Overview

Six threat-inducing experiments (total N = 233) testing the core HADD prediction: threat increases false-positive agent detection.

| Experiment | N | Threat Manipulation | Dependent Measure | Design |
|------------|---|---------------------|-------------------|--------|
| 1a | ~20 | Threatening pictures (IAPS) | Biological Motion Detection Task | Within-subjects |
| 1b | ~20 | Threatening pictures (IAPS) | Biological Motion Detection Task | Within-subjects |
| 2a | ~30 | Classical horror music | Biological Motion Detection Task | Within-subjects |
| 2b | ~60 | Classical horror music | Biological Motion Detection Task | Between-subjects |
| 3 | ~40 | Classical horror music | Geometrical Figures Task (intentionality) | Within-subjects |
| 4 | ~63 | Virtual reality (dark basement) | Auditory Agent Detection Task | Between-subjects |

## Threat Manipulations

1. **Experiments 1a, 1b**: International Affective Picture System (IAPS) — threat vs. neutral/positive pictures presented before each trial
2. **Experiments 2a, 2b, 3**: Classical horror music (e.g., Psycho soundtrack) vs. neutral music, played during the task
3. **Experiment 4**: Virtual reality — participants navigated a dark basement (threat condition) or a desert (control condition)

All manipulations classified as "weak" (pictures, music) to "moderate" (VR).

## Dependent Measures

### Biological Motion Detection Task (Exp 1a, 1b, 2a, 2b)
- Stimuli: 12-dot point-light walker on black background
- Independent variables: walker present/absent × 3 levels of visual noise (48, 96, 192 distractor dots)
- Task: judge whether a human stick figure (point-light walker) is present or absent
- Measures: hit rate, false alarm rate, d' (sensitivity), c (response bias)

### Geometrical Figures Task (Exp 3)
- Based on Heider & Simmel (1944) paradigm
- Stimuli: geometric shapes moving mechanistically, intentionally, or randomly
- Task: judge whether figures are moving intentionally
- Operationalization: false attribution of intentionality to random/mechanistic movements

### Auditory Agent Detection Task (Exp 4)
- Stimuli: human voice embedded in white noise at varying signal-to-noise ratios
- Task: detect presence/absence of a human voice
- VR context: dark basement (threat) vs. desert (control)

## Key Findings

### Primary Result: Null Effect of Threat on Agency Detection
Threat manipulations (weak to moderate) did **not** increase false detection of:
- Human agents (Biological Motion Detection Task, Experiments 1 & 2)
- Human voices (Auditory Agent Detection Task, Experiment 4)
- Intentionality (Geometrical Figures Task, Experiment 3)

### Response Bias: Conservative (Agent-Absent) Bias
Across Experiments 1a, 1b, 2a, 2b, and 4, participants showed a significant bias toward responding "agent absent" (c > 0, indicating conservative criterion). This directly contradicts the HADD prediction of a liberal "agent present" bias.

### Individual Differences: No Relationship with Supernatural Beliefs
- Agent detection measures were **unrelated** to scores on the Revised Paranormal Belief Scale (RPBS)
- No relationship with religiosity measures
- **Contradicts** prior findings (van Elk, 2013; van Elk et al., 2016) where paranormal believers showed more illusory agency detection

### Negativity Bias Correlation
- Agent and intentionality detection measures were **related to the negativity bias** (tendency to focus on negative stimuli)
- Suggests individual differences in threat sensitivity may matter more than supernatural beliefs

## Signal Detection Theory Analysis
- Used SDT throughout to separate sensitivity (d') from response bias (c)
- No threat effects on d' (perceptual sensitivity) in any experiment
- No threat effects on c (response bias) — bias was consistently conservative
- Methodology mirrors error management theory framework (Haselton & Nettle, 2006)

## Limitations (identified by authors)

1. **Weak threat manipulations**: "We used weak to moderate threat-eliciting manipulations" — may not have been strong enough to activate HADD
2. **Laboratory settings**: Computer tasks lack ecological validity of real threatening situations
3. **Human vs. supernatural agents**: Tasks measured human agent detection, not supernatural agent detection — HADD theory primarily concerns supernatural agents
4. **Sample characteristics**: Mostly undergraduate students; limited generalizability
5. **Demand characteristics**: Within-subjects designs may have revealed the manipulation intent

## Cross-Validation from Follow-up Research

### Andersen et al. (2019) / Replication Study (via ruj.uj.edu.pl)
- Preregistered VR study revisited whether threat increases false-positive agency detection
- **Did not confirm** that people are more likely to falsely detect agents when expecting danger
- "Casts doubt on HADD conceptualized as a threat-triggered mechanism"
- Suggests predictive processing (PPAD) may better account for agency detection

### Teehan (2024) — Toward an embodied cognitive science of religion
- Critically discusses Maij et al. findings
- Notes that ecological validity problems arise from cognitivist assumptions
- Points out that pressing keyboard buttons in response to point-light walkers bears little resemblance to real threat detection

## Implications for HADD Theory

1. **Core prediction fails**: Threat does not reliably increase false-positive agency detection under controlled conditions
2. **Boundary conditions identified**: HADD effects may be limited to:
   - Stronger threat manipulations (e.g., real-world danger)
   - Implicit measures (e.g., skin conductance, approach-avoidance)
   - Specific populations (e.g., high trait anxiety)
3. **Alternative frameworks**: Predictive processing (PPAD) may offer better explanatory power
4. **Paradigm shift needed**: Authors recommend VR and physiological measures for future research

## Key Quotes

> "Threat manipulations that were either weak (threatening pictures, classical horror music) or moderate (virtual reality) did not increase false human agent or intentionality detection."

> "Participants generally had a response bias towards assuming that agents were absent."

> "Agent and intentionality detection measures were unrelated to individual differences in supernatural beliefs, although they were related to the negativity bias."

## Credibility Assessment

- **Peer-reviewed** in Religion, Brain & Behavior (Taylor & Francis)
- **Open access** with CC BY-NC-ND license
- **Multiple experiments** (6) with converging null results
- **Signal detection theory** applied rigorously
- **Well-powered** aggregate (N = 233)
- **Limitations transparently discussed**
- **Pre-registration**: Not explicitly stated (pre-registration was less common in 2017 submission)
- **Replication attempts**: Null results have been replicated (Andersen et al.; Polish VR study)

Score: 85/100 — rigorous multi-experiment design, transparent reporting, converging evidence across modalities, but threat intensity limitations and lack of pre-registration reduce score.
