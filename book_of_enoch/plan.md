# Research Plan: Book of Enoch - Implications and Ideas

## Mode: Deep (8 phases, 10-20 min)

## Primary Sources
- 1 Enoch critical editions (Nickelsburg, Black, Knibb)
- Dead Sea Scrolls Aramaic fragments (4Q201-212)
- Ethiopic manuscript tradition
- Greek fragments (Akhmim, Chester Beatty)

## Secondary Sources
- G.W.E. Nickelsburg, 1 Enoch 1 (Hermeneia, 2001)
- G.W.E. Nickelsburg & J.C. VanderKam, 1 Enoch 2 (Hermeneia, 2012)
- J.C. VanderKam, Enoch and the Growth of an Apocalyptic Tradition (1984)
- J.J. Collins, The Apocalyptic Imagination (1998)
- M.A. Knibb, The Ethiopic Book of Enoch (1978)
- D. Dimant, "The Fallen Angels" (various)
- P.S. Alexander (3 Enoch)
- Andrei Orlov (2 Enoch, Enoch-Metatron)
- E. Isaac (OTP translation)

## Knowledge Dependencies
1. First: Understand text structure and composition history
2. Then: Explore individual thematic clusters
3. Then: Map influence and reception
4. Finally: Synthesize scholarly debates

## Triangulation Approach
- Compare Nickelsburg vs. Collins vs. VanderKam interpretations
- Cross-reference Dead Sea Scrolls evidence with Ethiopic tradition
- Balance "maximalist" vs. "minimalist" positions on NT influence

## Quality Gates
- 25+ sources before proceeding to synthesis
- 3+ independent sources per major claim
- Coverage of all 5 Enochic books
- Both proponent and critic voices on contested points
- Verified primary-text support for all thematic claims
